# Tea & Tech — Morning 1 Booklet

A5, 8 pages, saddle-stitched. Body 14pt minimum, headings 20–24pt. Cream paper, not white. One accent colour, used sparingly.

The booklet is a **companion**, not a manual. It exists so the learner has something in their hand during the morning, something to take home, and something to put on the fridge. Nobody should need to read it cover-to-cover to follow the morning — the host is doing that work.

## Page 2 — What is Claude?

> Claude is a helper that lives on your phone. You can talk to it, and it talks back — in writing, mostly, like a very patient pen-friend.
>
> It is **not** the internet. It is **not** Google. It is **not** a person.
>
> It can read a letter for you, write a message for you, explain a word, suggest a recipe, tell you what a strange text means. It can be wrong, and sometimes it is. You are always the one who decides what to do.
>
> Today, you'll get it onto your phone, and have your first conversation with it. That's the whole job.

*Small illustration: a teacup beside a phone. Steam rising. Nothing busier than that.*

## Page 3 — Installing Claude (iPhone)

Three screenshots down the page, with one line under each.

1. **Open the App Store.** The blue icon with the white "A".
2. **Tap the search box. Press the little microphone, and say "Claude".**
3. **Tap GET on the one with the orange-and-cream icon.** It will ask for your password or your fingerprint. That's normal.

> If your phone won't let you install it, don't worry. Hold your hand up — we'll sort it.

## Page 5 — Talking to Claude

The most important page. Big and uncluttered.

> **The microphone is the trick.**
>
> When Claude is open, you'll see a box at the bottom of the screen. Inside the box, on the right, is a **little microphone**.
>
> 1. **Press the microphone.**
> 2. **Wait for the little wave to appear.**
> 3. **Say what you'd like to ask. In your own voice. Pause when you're done.**
> 4. **Press the microphone again, or the arrow.**
>
> Claude will think for a moment. Then it'll write back.

*Illustration: a phone screen with the microphone circled. An arrow pointing at the wave.*

---

## Page 6 — Three good ways to ask

> If the answer is too long, too short, or just not quite right — **say so.** It's a conversation, not a search.
>
> **"Explain it like I'm new to this."**
> Use this when a word or an idea has gone over your head.
>
> **"Make it shorter."**
> Use this when Claude has written you a small novel and you wanted a sentence.
>
> **"Try again — that wasn't quite right."**
> Use this when the answer missed the point. You don't need to be polite about it. (Though you can be. Many people are. It doesn't mind either way.)

---

## Page 7 — Three things never to share

> Claude is helpful. It is also a stranger. Treat it the way you'd treat a friendly person on the bus.
>
> **Never share your PPS number.**
> No genuine helper needs it to answer a question.
>
> **Never share your card numbers.**
> Not your bank card, not your credit card, not the long number on the back.
>
> **Never share your passwords.**
> Not for your email, your bank, your pension, anything.
>
> If anyone — Claude or otherwise — *asks* for these, that itself is the warning sign. Close the app and ring someone you trust.

## Production notes

- **No page numbers.** Eight pages doesn't need them, and they add visual clutter.
- **No table of contents.** Same reason.
- **One illustration per spread, max.** Cover, page 5, page 8. Anywhere else, white space.
- **Print enough.** 1 per learner, 5 spare per morning, 5 for the venue noticeboard / library / sponsor.
- **The booklet is the artefact.** Get this one right and the next three mornings inherit the template.
