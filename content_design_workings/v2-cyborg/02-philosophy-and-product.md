# Cyborg — v2 Philosophy and Product Concept
*Date: 2026-04-15*

---

## The Name: Cyborg

Cyborg is not a science-fiction word in this context. It is a description of a working state: a human whose thinking, output, and leverage are materially extended by machines they actively direct. The name signals that this is about augmentation, not replacement. The human is still the one thinking, choosing, and taking responsibility — the machine is grafted on to multiply what they can reach.

Linguist v1 was a reassurance product. Its implicit audience was a nervous professional who needed permission to press send. "Cyborg" breaks that frame on purpose. It excludes the learner who wants to be told AI is safe, easy, and basically a nicer Google. It includes the learner who is already a little suspicious of how much leverage this gives the people using it well — and wants in. The emotional register is deliberate: slightly more intense than comfortable, but not cultish. Competent. Adult. A little dangerous in a way the learner wants to be associated with.

The teaching philosophy encoded in the name: a cyborg is not made by reading about prosthetics. They are made by spending time wearing them until the joint between the human part and the machine part becomes unconscious. The course exists to shorten that integration period from years to weeks. Everything in it is calibrated toward that single outcome — not knowledge, not confidence, but integration.

Is the name right? It is right for the audience that v2 is for, which is not the full v1 audience. If the commercial model still needs a mass-market entry ramp, "Linguist" can remain the front door and "Cyborg" can be the core product — the place the serious learner ends up. The name does the segmentation work for us.

---

## The Core Thesis

**Every capable person now has a choice: keep working at human scale, or rewire how they work so the machine does the parts that scale and they do the parts that only a human can do. Cyborg teaches that rewiring as a practical craft, scenario by scenario, until the learner stops noticing the seam between their thinking and the machine's output.** The course is not about using AI better. It is about the specific, learnable skill of redesigning your own work and thinking processes around the fact that a capable model is available on tap — and knowing, in each moment, whether this is a moment to think out loud with the machine (Human Mode) or to instruct it precisely (Machine Mode). The claim is that the gap between people who can do this and people who cannot is about to become the single largest productivity differential in knowledge work, and that the skill is teachable if taught concretely enough.

## The Target Audience

v1 tried to serve everyone. v2 serves one person and does it well.

**The primary audience is the ambitious high-agency operator.** They already have a job, a craft, or a business. They are technical-adjacent — not necessarily a developer, but someone who builds spreadsheets that work, writes documents that get used, runs processes that other people depend on. They have tried AI. They have had moments where it felt like a superpower and moments where it wasted an hour of their day, and they cannot yet predict which is which. They suspect that the people getting the most out of AI are not smarter than they are — they have just figured out the pattern, and they want the pattern.

They are not beginners in the "I am scared of computers" sense. They are beginners in the sense that nobody has yet shown them how AI integrates with real work, not toy examples. They are willing to pay and willing to do work; they are not willing to sit through reassurance.

**Is there a beginner entry point?** Yes, but the beginner track in v2 has a different character from v1's B-track. It is not "do not be scared of the send button." It is "here are five scenarios from your actual work life and here is what a Cyborg version of each one looks like." The beginner is treated as a competent adult who happens to not yet know the moves. The anxiety-first framing of v1 is dropped. Nervous learners are still welcome, but the course does not slow down for them — it gives them the shortest possible path to a first real win and then keeps moving.

**What "superpowered" actually means in practice.** A Cyborg-trained person can:
- Take a messy real-life situation (a vague client brief, a personal decision, a team problem) and, within ten minutes, have a usefully structured view of it — because they know how to think out loud with AI and then extract the signal.
- Turn a recurring task that currently takes them an hour into a five-minute operation with a standing instruction, a brief, or a small automation — not once, but as a habit.
- Detect within three or four exchanges when an AI output is wrong, thin, hallucinated, or over-confident, and correct course without losing the thread.
- Chain tools together (AI plus automation plus scheduling plus storage) so that entire slices of their work happen without their attention.
- Switch between exploratory (Human Mode) and execution (Machine Mode) use of AI without friction — which is the skill v1 identified but did not teach.

**How the automation angle shapes who this is for.** The combination of AI + workflow automation (Zapier, Make, n8n, scripts, scheduled agents, webhooks, simple APIs) is what takes a person from "uses AI well" to "uses AI at scale." v2 teaches that combination. That means the audience tilts toward people who are willing to touch a workflow tool, paste a webhook URL, read a simple JSON response. It is not a developer course. It is a course for the professional who is ready to stop doing things by hand if they can help it.

## The Teaching Philosophy

v1 taught abstractions with examples attached. v2 inverts this. Every lesson starts with a scenario, shows the Cyborg move, and then — only after the learner has seen the move work — names the pattern underneath. Meat and potatoes. Ready-to-apply. No theory without a use.

### What a v2 micro-lesson looks like

Five beats. Ten minutes end to end. Always the same shape so the form disappears.

1. **Scenario (30 seconds).** A specific situation the target profile recognises. "You are a founder who just got a 40-minute call transcript from a customer interview and you have 15 minutes before your next meeting."
2. **The move (2 minutes).** Here is what a Cyborg does with that situation. Shown, not explained. The prompt, the output, the follow-up, the result. A real demo of the pattern, not a sanitised example.
3. **The pattern (1 minute).** One line of generalisation. "This is a Machine Mode extraction — precise verb, specific shape, one pass." No longer than that. The pattern is named, not taught.
4. **Your turn (5 minutes).** A Copy → Personalise → Use exercise on the learner's own material. If they do not have material handy, a plausible substitute is offered. The exercise has to produce a tangible output they can reuse.
5. **The tell (1 minute).** One bullshit-detection cue specific to this move. "If the output includes a phrase from the transcript that is not actually in your transcript, it is hallucinating — stop and re-seed." One tell per lesson. Cumulative.

Total: one scenario, one move, one pattern, one reusable artefact, one detection cue. No more.

### Profiles

v2 uses five profiles. Every lesson runs in whichever profile the learner has selected (the profile system already exists in v1 and works). The profiles are chosen to span the actual audience, not to be exhaustive.

1. **The Operator.** A manager, ops person, or internal functional lead. They live in documents, meetings, and processes. They have recurring work that could be automated but is not.
2. **The Founder.** Running something small. Wears every hat. Shortest on time, highest willingness to pay for leverage. Context-switches constantly.
3. **The Specialist.** A deep expert in one domain (legal, clinical, financial, creative). They care about accuracy above all and have the strongest bullshit-detection instincts. Their Cyborg training is mostly about trust calibration.
4. **The Builder.** Technical-adjacent. Can write a Zap, maybe a script. Wants to chain things together and have slices of their work run without them.
5. **The Polymath.** Works across fields and formats. Writes, analyses, researches, makes. Their Cyborg training is about switching modes rapidly and managing parallel threads without losing coherence.

Each lesson's scenario reads differently by profile but teaches the same underlying move. The learner sees their version.

### How Human Mode / Machine Mode gets taught through scenarios

The two modes are never introduced as theory. They are introduced as two scenes the learner watches back-to-back in Module 1.

**Scene 1 (Human Mode):** The Founder profile has a weird feeling about a new hire decision. Cannot articulate why. Opens a chat, types "I need to think through something — give me a sec," and then starts typing what they are noticing. The AI asks questions. Ten messages later, the Founder has named the concern. They close the chat. No output. The thinking is the output.

**Scene 2 (Machine Mode):** Same Founder, next morning. Knows exactly what they need: a 200-word message to the hiring manager explaining the pause, professional tone, no accusatory language, three specific concerns listed. One precise prompt. One clean response. Done in ninety seconds.

Then the lesson names them: these are the two modes. You have just seen both. The rest of the course will teach you when each one is right and how to move between them. That is the framing. No diagrams. No two-column tables. Two scenes.

The transition — the skill nobody teaches — gets its own module early on, built around a single exercise: take a Human Mode conversation you just had and convert what you learned into a Machine Mode Opening Seed. The learner does this once, feels the switch, and has it for life.

### How bullshit-detection gets taught

As a running thread, not a module. Every lesson ends with one "tell" — a specific, concrete cue that this kind of output has failed. Cumulatively the learner builds a detection vocabulary that is tied to actual scenarios, not abstract warnings about hallucination.

There is also one dedicated module — **Calibrating Trust** — that teaches the generalised skill: when to verify, what to verify against, how much effort to spend verifying given the stakes of the task. But the habit is built across every lesson.

The bullshit-detection frame is not about fear. It is about professional competence. A doctor who cannot tell when a test result is wrong is not a doctor. A Cyborg who cannot tell when the machine is wrong is not a Cyborg.

### Lesson length and density

Hard ceiling: ten minutes to complete, including the exercise. Readable in five. The density is controlled not by cutting content but by cutting frame — no lengthy introductions, no "what is happening" essays, no recap of previous modules. The scenario carries the weight.

v1's modules averaged much longer and were more cognitively diffuse. v2 trades depth-per-lesson for lessons-per-week. A learner who does one Cyborg micro-lesson a day for a month has done thirty moves. That is enough to be dangerous.

Cognitive overload is managed by three rules:
- One new move per lesson. Not two.
- One new piece of vocabulary per lesson. Not two.
- Every lesson produces one reusable artefact the learner keeps. Over time this artefact collection becomes their personal Cyborg toolkit — which is the actual object the course is building.

## The Commercial Architecture

v1's model: free course on the web, paywalled sandbox via Substack subscription. The commercial side was grafted onto a free content product. It worked as proof of concept but the commercial and philosophical sides were disconnected — what you paid for was not the best version of what the course was trying to teach.

v2 reconnects them.

### Free tier: The Scenario Library

Free forever. Not a course — a library. A browsable, searchable collection of Cyborg scenarios by profile and by situation. "I have a 40-minute transcript and 15 minutes" is a scenario. "I need to write an awkward message and I am stalling" is a scenario. "My inbox has 200 unread and I need to triage" is a scenario. Each one is a 10-minute Cyborg move with a template.

The library is free because it builds trust and demonstrates the value proposition. It is also the best discovery surface: people search for their specific situation and land on a Cyborg move. Every scenario contains a soft path to the paid product ("This is move #3 in the Inbox Triage Cyborg Path — if you want the full sequence, join Cyborg").

This replaces v1's free course. It is higher leverage because it is indexed by situation, not by curriculum order.

### Paid tier: Cyborg

A single paid product. One price. Includes:

- **The Core Paths.** Four or five curated sequences of about twenty lessons each, structured by profile. The Operator's Path. The Founder's Path. And so on. These are the structured learning experience — meat and potatoes, one move per lesson, scenario-first.
- **The Template Pack.** Every move has a matching, copy-paste template with the five profile variants filled in. This is the artefact v1's template pack brainstorm pointed toward. In v2 it is not an adjunct — it is the core deliverable. The templates are the product. The lessons are how you learn to use them.
- **The Automation Recipes.** Every Cyborg Path culminates in one or two small automations — Zap, Make scenario, scheduled agent, script — that operationalise the moves. Pre-built, documented, and ready to fork.
- **The Sandbox.** A working repo that demonstrates the combined workflow — the Substack-documented, in-progress Vuelo Labs build. Kept. Updated. It is the proof that the course teaches real work.
- **Updates.** New scenarios, new paths, new recipes as the landscape changes. This is the recurring-value justification.

### Pricing and model

A subscription is the right model because the surface is always moving and the value of staying current is real. Price at the "serious tool" tier — high enough to attract the high-agency audience, low enough to be a no-brainer for anyone who would gain back even an hour a week. The pricing signals what the product is: not a course, an ongoing membership in a way of working.

A one-time "lifetime access" option at a meaningful multiple of the annual price is worth offering for the segment that hates subscriptions. They are the most committed learners and the best word-of-mouth.

### Self-teaching vs cohort vs coaching

v1's self-teaching format was right for its audience (nervous beginners who wanted anonymity) but is wrong for Cyborg's audience. Ambitious people want signal that others are doing the work with them. They want a reason to actually complete it. But they do not want a schedule imposed on them.

The right compromise: **async cohort.** Every scenario and path can be done at any time, but there is a rolling weekly rhythm — a newsletter that highlights one scenario, a community thread where people post their artefacts, a monthly founder-led workshop on a specific Cyborg move. The cohort feeling is real, the commitment is flexible. This is how the most successful paid learning products now operate.

Live cohorts and 1:1 coaching can exist as premium tiers on top, but are not the default. The default is self-paced inside a visible community rhythm.

## What Cyborg Is Not

v2 gets its identity partly by what it refuses to be. The exclusions:

- **Not an AI literacy course.** The course assumes the learner has used AI at least a few times. It does not explain what a large language model is. It does not compare GPT to Claude to Gemini in the abstract. It uses whichever tool is best for the move and gets on with it.
- **Not a prompt engineering course.** Prompts are means, not ends. v1 was structurally a prompting course and the word "prompt" did a lot of load-bearing work. v2 almost never uses the word. The unit is the move, not the prompt.
- **Not a tools tour.** There will be no module called "10 AI Tools You Should Know." Tools are mentioned when they are the right tool for a move. The appendix can be a landscape reference, but it is not the spine of the course.
- **Not for everyone.** The nervous beginner who needs permission to press send is better served by the free Scenario Library and by v1's remaining content. v2 does not apologise for its pace.
- **Not a replacement for judgement.** Every Cyborg move preserves the human's authority over the decision. The course refuses any framing that suggests the AI should decide. Delegation has limits and v2 names them explicitly, especially for the Specialist profile.
- **Not a community-first product.** The community exists to support the work. It is not the product. People do not pay to belong; they pay to become more capable. The community is an amplifier of that, not a substitute.
- **Not cohort-gated.** The learner can start at any moment, on their own material, in their own rhythm. The rhythm comes from outside; the commitment is internal.
- **Not evergreen in the pretend sense.** The landscape moves. v2 commits to updating rather than to eternal stability. That is part of what the subscription buys.

## The Emotional Arc

v1 was reassuring throughout. It handed the learner a pillow at the start and kept offering more pillows.

v2 has a different emotional contract. The learner should feel:

**At the start: recognised and slightly provoked.** The first scenario should hit a nerve — "yes, that is exactly the situation I am bad at." Not a reassurance that they are fine. A tiny flash of embarrassment that they have been solving this the hard way for months. Followed immediately by the Cyborg move, which resolves the embarrassment into curiosity. *I have been doing this the long way. There is a short way. Show me.*

**In the early middle: momentum and a little greed.** By the fifth or sixth scenario, the learner has a growing collection of moves and templates that are actually saving them time. They start seeing situations at work and thinking "that is a Cyborg move." They start wanting more. This is the phase where they upgrade from free to paid, or from paid to fully engaged. The dominant feeling is *I want the next one.*

**In the late middle: a difficulty spike that matters.** Somewhere around the automation content or the trust-calibration module, the learner hits something they cannot just copy-paste. A webhook does not fire. A Claude Code session costs more than expected. An AI output is confidently wrong in a way they almost missed. This is deliberate. The course would be lying if it promised only ease. The feeling here is *this is harder than the template suggested — and it matters that I get it right.* They push through because the earlier wins have bought trust.

**At the end: recomposed.** Not triumphant. Not graduated. Recomposed. The learner notices, one day, that they did something in twenty minutes that would have taken them two hours six weeks ago, and they did not think about AI the whole time — they just worked. The seam is gone. That is the actual outcome. The course should end by naming it: *you are now doing this unconsciously. That was the whole point. Here is what to do with the time you have gained.* The emotional register is competent, quiet, slightly dangerous. Adult. Ready.

The arc in one line: **recognition → momentum → challenge → integration.** That is what being made into a Cyborg feels like, and it is what the course has to deliver — not just describe.
