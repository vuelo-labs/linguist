# Cyborg — Mission
*Date: 2026-04-16*

---

## The Mission

**Teach ambitious people to rewire how they work so the machine handles what scales and they handle what only a human can do — until the seam between them disappears.**

That is the whole thing. Every content decision, product decision, and pricing decision should be answerable by: *does this move someone toward that outcome, or away from it?*

---

## Where This Came From

Linguist v1 was built on a genuine insight from `LINGUIST_BUILD_CONTEXT.md`:

> Humans are spending 40–50+ hours a week talking to machines using the same emotional, social, and performative patterns they use with other humans. This is causing cognitive burnout — not from the work, but from the mode mismatch.

The original product was a reassurance product. The two absurdities (apologising to a chatbot, dispatching a human like a machine) were the teaching moment. The audience was people burning out from mode confusion — nervous, compliance-driven, low general tech confidence. "The machine will wait. It does not mind. Go be human."

That insight is still true. The two modes are still the spine.

**What changed** is who the course is for. The v2 Cyborg documents make a deliberate break: the target is not the nervous professional who needs permission to press send. It is the high-agency operator who already suspects there is a pattern to the people getting the most out of AI — and wants it.

The emotional register shifted from reassurance to competence. The promise shifted from "you won't burn out" to "you will operate at a scale that would have been impossible alone."

Both are honest. They are different products for different people.

---

## The Core Tension: Who Is This For?

This is the decision that everything else follows from. It is not yet fully resolved.

### Option A — The original audience, better taught

The v1 audience was right but the product was wrong. Nervous beginners, compliance-driven professionals, people who apologise to chatbots. The Human/Machine Mode frame is exactly what they need — it names their confusion and gives them a way out. The problem was v1 taught one mode and called it two.

A v2 built for this audience would:
- Keep the reassurance tone of `/see/`
- Slow down at the mode-recognition skill before accelerating into Machine Mode technique
- Have a genuine free entry ramp (the Scenario Library)
- Stop before automation — not everyone needs a Zapier flow
- Be the most mission-aligned version of the product

**Commercial challenge:** this audience does not buy 27-module courses. The delivery shape has to change even if the audience stays the same.

### Option B — The Cyborg audience: high-agency operators

The v2 Cyborg documents make this call explicitly. Ambitious, technical-adjacent, already tried AI, ready for leverage. The Returner profile acknowledges this is sometimes a returning v1 learner, not always a new one.

A v2 built for this audience would:
- Open with recognition and slight provocation, not reassurance
- Move fast, assume competence, spend most of the course on automation
- Have the automation layer (Stage 5) as the primary commercial value, not a bonus
- Price at the "serious tool" tier
- Produce Cyborgs, not enabled users

**Commercial challenge:** smaller addressable market. The people who are ready for this course self-select. Word of mouth is strong; top-of-funnel is thinner.

### Option C — Both, with a handoff

Linguist stays the front door (free, accessible, the original audience). Cyborg is the place you end up if you want to go further. The Scenario Library serves the v1 audience at no cost. Cyborg is for the fraction who are ready for augmentation.

This is what the v2 philosophy doc gestures at:
> "If the commercial model still needs a mass-market entry ramp, 'Linguist' can remain the front door and 'Cyborg' can be the core product."

**Challenge:** two products, not one. Higher build cost, more ambiguous positioning. The risk is doing both halfheartedly.

## The Decision Liam Needs to Make

Before writing a single lesson, one question needs a committed answer:

> **Who is the primary paying audience: the nervous professional who needs the mode-switch explained, or the ambitious operator who wants to move from capable to leveraged?**

This is not a content question. It is a commercial question. The answer changes:
- The emotional register of every lesson
- The profiles used
- Where the course starts and ends
- What the paid tier contains
- How it is priced and positioned
- What success looks like for a completed learner

Both are viable. Option C (handoff model) is viable but requires committing to building two things properly, not one thing halfheartedly.

The v2 Cyborg documents already lean hard into Option B. If that is the call, commit to it now and stop hedging toward the v1 audience. If Option C is the call, that needs to be designed explicitly — not assumed.

---

## What Is Not in Dispute

Regardless of which audience decision is made, these are settled:

1. **The Human/Machine Mode frame is the spine.** Not a module. Not a chapter. The single organising principle of everything.

2. **Scenario-first is the teaching method.** Theory follows experience. The learner lives the problem before hearing its name.

3. **Copy → Personalise → Use is the exercise pattern.** Unchanged from v1. It is the only way templates land for learners who are not confident enough to start from scratch.

4. **"Never delegate understanding" is the course's ethical spine.** Planted in lesson 3. Quoted at the capstone. Non-negotiable.

5. **The `/see/` experience is preserved.** The two absurdities are the entry moment. Whether v2 rebuilds it exactly or references it as a prerequisite, it stays.

6. **Bullshit detection is not optional.** Whatever the audience, they need to know when the machine is wrong. Placed after fluency, before automation.

7. **The automation layer is what v1 never had.** Even for the v1 audience at low depth — persistent instructions are the softest form of automation and every learner should reach them.

8. **The product commits to updating.** The landscape moves. Pretending otherwise breaks trust. The subscription model exists to make updating sustainable.

---

## The Mission Statement, Applied

**The course succeeds when a learner does something in twenty minutes that would have taken them two hours before — and doesn't think about AI the whole time. The seam is gone. That is the outcome.**

Every planning decision should be tested against that. If a module does not move the learner toward seamless integration, it is scaffolding, not teaching. Cut it or fold it.
