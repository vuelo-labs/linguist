## E-01: The First Word Matters

**Module concept:** The opening verb determines how much authority the AI assumes over the task — safe verbs set a defined ceiling, risky verbs delegate judgment.

---

### Idea 1: The Verb Autopsy

**Format:** Annotated side-by-side transcript (printable / pasteable comparison doc)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
The learner takes one real piece of their own work — an email, a paragraph, a message they've written. They send it to the AI three times with three different opening verbs: "Improve," "Rewrite," "Fix." They paste all three responses into a simple three-column layout. Then they annotate: circle every change the AI made that they didn't ask for. In the "Improve" column, most circles will be in parts they didn't touch. In the "Fix" column, almost none.

The annotation step is load-bearing — reading the difference isn't enough. The physical act of circling makes the AI's assumed authority visible in a way that just comparing text doesn't.

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The module tells you the difference. The autopsy makes you see it in your own words, your own text, your own unwanted changes. The aha is: "Oh — it restructured this paragraph because 'improve' gave it permission to. I never would have done that intentionally." Generic examples can't produce this. Your own text can.

---

### Idea 2: The Permission Dial

**Format:** Visual reference card (single image, print-friendly)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
A horizontal dial from "AI decides nothing" to "AI decides everything." Safe verbs (Fix, Summarise, List) sit at the left end with a short note: "You set the scope." Risky verbs (Improve, Enhance, Optimise) sit at the right with a note: "AI sets the scope." Middle-ground verbs (Rewrite, Draft) sit in the middle with notes showing what tips them left or right (adding constraints moves them left; no constraints moves them right).

The user doesn't just read it — they place their last three prompts on the dial and circle where they landed. Did they mean to hand that much over?

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The module uses the metaphor of a "ceiling." The dial makes the same concept spatial and comparative — you can see that "Improve" and "Optimise" are at the same end for the same reason. It also gives learners a quick diagnostic tool they'll reach for after the course. The placement exercise forces reflection on actual past behavior, not a hypothetical.

---

### Idea 3: The Verb Swap Drill

**Format:** Guided exercise (5 minutes, one real task)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
A pre-written "bad" prompt using a risky verb is given to the learner. Then they're given the same prompt with a blank where the verb should be and five slots for constraints. Task: pick a safer verb and fill in at least one constraint. Then send both. The catch — the "bad" prompt and the "repaired" prompt use the same text, so the only variables are the verb and the constraint. Learners then answer three diagnostic questions about what changed.

Crucially: the pre-written bad prompt uses a recognisably frustrating real-world scenario — "Make this email better" before a job application — so the stakes feel real even though the prompt is provided.

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The module's own exercise is good but asks learners to generate everything themselves, which can let people write safe prompts they don't actually have to defend. A provided "bad" prompt removes that escape hatch. You have to repair something that's objectively loose. The diagnosis questions — "what changed and why" — force articulation of the principle, which deepens retention.

---

**Preferred pick: Idea 1 (The Verb Autopsy)**

The autopsy wins because it uses the learner's own text, which means the unwanted changes are personal. "It changed my sign-off" hits harder than "it might change your sign-off." The module's exercise already has this instinct — Part 1 asks learners to use their own work — but the annotation step adds a physical act of discovery that the reading-and-comparing instruction doesn't. The circles make implicit AI decision-making visible.

**Concerns:**
The autopsy works best when the learner's original text is good enough that the AI's "improvements" are clearly unwanted. If the original text is genuinely poor, the AI's rewrites might look like improvements and obscure the lesson. Worth noting in the facilitator notes: use something you're proud of.

---

## LOG — E2 — E-01

Strong aha potential here. The verb-as-permission-grant is the sharpest conceptual move in the whole track — it's the reframe from "what do I want" to "how much am I handing over." The best supplementary content will make that visible in personal, concrete terms. The autopsy does that; the dial is more reference than insight. Idea 3 is solid but slightly gameable. No stretching needed — this module is rich ground.

## E-02: Tell It What to Leave Alone

**Module concept:** A constraint tells the AI where its authority ends — without it, the AI treats "do this job" as permission to improve anything adjacent to the job.

---

### Idea 1: The Collateral Damage Audit

**Format:** Structured comparison worksheet (one page)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
The learner sends a constrained and unconstrained version of the same prompt (the module's own exercise already prompts this). But then — and this is the extension — they're asked to catalogue what changed in the unconstrained version by type, using the five constraint categories from the module: scope, style, additions, format, voice. They tick which categories the AI touched without being asked.

The worksheet has two columns: "What I asked for" vs "What it also did." Under the second column, every entry is categorised: scope / style / addition / format / voice.

Then: "Which one of these hurt? If any of it didn't bother you, why not?"

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The module introduces five constraint types abstractly. The worksheet forces the learner to assign real AI behaviour to real categories. Most learners will find that the AI's unsolicited changes cluster in one or two categories — and that those are the same categories they care about most. "Oh — it's always format and additions for me. I don't care about style." That personalisation of the constraint vocabulary is something the module text can't produce on its own.

---

### Idea 2: The Negative Space Rewrite

**Format:** Guided rewrite exercise (15 minutes)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
The learner is given a poorly-constrained prompt from a realistic scenario — something like "Clean up this meeting summary" — and asked to identify everything in the document that they would want left exactly as-is. Not what they want changed. What they want protected. They make a list: three things that should survive untouched.

Then they write the constraint layer only — not the full prompt, just the "Don't" lines. They have to write at least two constraints, using the vocabulary from the module (scope, style, additions, format, voice).

Then they attach those constraints to the original prompt and send it.

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The key insight the module is driving toward is: you have to think about what to protect before you send, not after you see what got changed. This exercise reverses the usual direction — instead of "here's my task, what's the constraint?" it's "here's what I'm protecting, now write the constraint." That inversion surfaces how much learners have implicitly assumed the AI knows what they value. The exercise makes the protective thinking explicit before the damage is done.

---

### Idea 3: Constraint Failure Gallery

**Format:** Short annotated case study set (3–4 examples)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
Three or four short "before and after" cases where an unconstrained prompt produced a response that technically completed the task but damaged something in the original. Each case shows: the original text, the prompt, the AI's response, and a one-line annotation of what was lost.

Examples: a formal letter where "rewrite to be clearer" produced a casual tone; a project update where "summarise the key points" cut the section the reader most needed; a job application where "tighten this up" removed a specific achievement.

For each case, the learner writes the constraint that would have prevented the damage — one "Don't" line per case.

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
Reading about constraint failure is abstract. Seeing three real cases and writing the prevention for each produces a different kind of understanding — "I would have sent this prompt without the constraint too." The gallery creates empathy with the mistake before the learner makes it, which is more useful than reading about what could go wrong.

---

**Preferred pick: Idea 2 (The Negative Space Rewrite)**

The conceptual move in this module is reversed-direction thinking — you have to name what you're protecting before you send, not after you've seen what was damaged. Idea 2 forces that reversal explicitly by asking learners to think in negative space first. The other two ideas are both good, but they work in the forward direction (send, then audit what went wrong). Idea 2 builds the protective instinct before the send, which is what the module is really trying to teach.

**Concerns:**
The "make a list of three things to protect" step might feel abstract for learners who don't have a strong sense of what they care about in their own work. It may help to give a worked example first — show someone's list, then ask the learner to make their own. Otherwise learners with low writing confidence may draw a blank.

---

## LOG — E2 — E-02

Good aha potential, but the module already does a lot of its own heavy lifting — Part 1 asks learners to send a constrained and unconstrained version and observe the difference. The supplementary content needs to add something the exercise doesn't already give you. The Collateral Damage Audit extends the observation with categorisation, which is useful but incremental. The Negative Space Rewrite adds a genuinely new cognitive move (protect first, prompt second), which is the insight the module aims at but doesn't quite land on its own. The Constraint Failure Gallery is solid but feels like supporting material for a different kind of course. No stretching, but the best idea is doing real work.

## E-03: Context Is Fuel

**Module concept:** Context is only useful if removing it would force the AI to guess — load-bearing context changes the output; decorative context doesn't.

---

### Idea 1: The Load-Bearing Test Live

**Format:** Guided exercise with prediction element (20 minutes)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
The learner takes a real prompt they've sent recently — one they felt gave reasonable context. They break it into individual pieces of context (one per line). For each piece, they make a prediction: "If I removed this, would the AI's response change significantly? Yes / No / Unsure."

Then they actually test it. For two or three of the pieces they marked "Yes" (load-bearing), they send the prompt without that piece and compare the outputs.

The final step is a calibration check: were their predictions right? For any "Yes" that turned out to be decorative, or any "No" that turned out to matter — why did they get it wrong?

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The module introduces the load-bearing test as a pre-send question. This exercise makes it a measurable prediction. The act of being wrong — "I thought my industry context was load-bearing, but the AI produced almost the same thing without it" — is the aha. The module can tell you what load-bearing means, but only failure at the prediction step can calibrate your intuition. That calibration is the thing worth having.

---

### Idea 2: The Context Stripping Sequence

**Format:** Structured live experiment (5 sends, same task)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
Start with a maximally context-heavy prompt for a real task — everything you know about the situation. Then strip it back one layer at a time, sending the same task with progressively less context. Each send removes one category (audience, then tone, then key facts, then purpose). Keep the verb and the task identical.

Five sends total. One per context layer. Compare responses: at what point does the output go generic? What was the last piece of context removed before the quality dropped?

That piece is the single most load-bearing fact in this task.

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The module's load-bearing test is a reasoning exercise. The stripping sequence is empirical. Learners find out by observation, not by thinking it through — and the sequence makes it impossible to miss the moment quality drops. "It stayed good until I removed the tone instruction. That one line was doing all the work." That specificity of discovery is not available from reading about it.

---

### Idea 3: The Context Swap

**Format:** Partner or self-comparison exercise (two versions of one prompt)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
The learner writes two versions of the same prompt for the same task: one with their real context (role, audience, key fact, tone) and one with a completely different context (a different role, a different audience, the same verb and task). Not "no context" — different context. The task stays identical.

For example: the same instruction "Draft a 3-paragraph project update, 150 words, no bullet points" — once for a marketing coordinator reporting to the CEO, once for an IT manager reporting to the operations team.

Send both. Read both responses side by side.

The point: the task was identical. The context changed everything.

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The module's contrast (with context vs without context) already demonstrates that context matters. The swap goes further — it shows that context is directional, not just present or absent. Two entirely different responses from the same verb proves that the context is the actual instruction, not the task statement. That's a sharper version of the module's main claim, and most learners won't fully believe it until they've seen it themselves.

---

**Preferred pick: Idea 1 (The Load-Bearing Test Live)**

The module's most important gift is a decision tool — the load-bearing test. Idea 1 takes that tool and makes it falsifiable. Prediction + test + calibration is a tighter learning loop than observation alone. The moment when a learner's prediction fails is the most instructive moment in the whole module — "I thought that sentence was load-bearing, but the AI barely used it." The stripping sequence (Idea 2) is compelling but requires five sends, which may be too much friction. The Context Swap (Idea 3) is elegant but relies on learners constructing a believable alternative context, which can feel manufactured.

**Concerns:**
The live test requires the learner to actually send modified prompts and compare outputs, which takes 15–20 minutes and a working AI session. For learners with lower engagement this may not happen. Consider pairing this with a worked example showing someone's predictions and the results — so learners can see what "wrong prediction" looks like before they try it themselves.

---

## LOG — E2 — E-03

The load-bearing test is genuinely elegant and the module lands it well. The supplementary content challenge here is: the module already has a strong "before and after" exercise (Part 2). What the exercises don't do is make the learner commit to a prediction first. That prediction step is the thing that turns passive observation into active calibration. Ideas 1 and 2 both use this empirical approach; Idea 1 adds prediction, Idea 2 doesn't. Idea 3 is the sharpest conceptual extension of the module's core claim (context is the real instruction) but depends on execution. No stretching here — the aha potential is clear.

## E-04: Restate, Don't Patch

**Module concept:** When corrections are accumulating without improving the response, the conversation itself has become the problem — a single well-formed restate beats continued patching every time.

---

### Idea 1: The Correction Spiral Transcript

**Format:** Annotated transcript (provided example for analysis)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
A provided fictional conversation transcript — 7 messages long — in which someone tries to get a good result through correction. The task is straightforward (draft a short explanation of a product feature). The person keeps patching: "actually make it less formal," "shorter please," "but keep the detail about the pricing." Each message is a reasonable correction. The final response is worse than the third one.

The learner annotates the transcript by marking: the moment the spiral started (when did patching stop improving the result?), what the original message was missing, and writing the single restate message that would have replaced all seven.

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The module describes the spiral well, but watching it happen to someone else in slow motion — with annotation markers — produces a different kind of recognition. "That third message was already too late — they should have stopped here." Learners recognise the pattern as a spectator first, which makes it easier to spot in their own conversations. Writing the restate at the end produces the satisfying "it was this simple" moment.

---

### Idea 2: The Message Length Test

**Format:** Self-diagnostic exercise (5 minutes, no AI needed)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
The learner opens a recent conversation they had with an AI. They measure message length across the conversation — roughly, in lines or sentences. They graph it: first message, second message, third message, etc.

The question: is the graph going up or down?

If it's going up, the messages are getting longer — usually a correction spiral. If it's going down or flat, the conversation is working.

They then pick the message where the length started increasing. That's the spiral start. What was the first correction? What was missing from the original message that made the correction necessary?

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The module gives three diagnostic signals for a correction spiral. This exercise makes one of them (message length) visible and measurable, with no AI interaction required. The graph doesn't lie. Seeing your own messages getting progressively longer is visceral proof that the conversation was working against you — more convincing than reading that it might. This also trains learners to self-monitor in real time without needing an external audit.

---

### Idea 3: Restate vs Patch Head-to-Head

**Format:** Parallel experiment (two live AI sessions)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
The learner picks a real task they've been struggling with — something that's taken multiple correction rounds in the past, or something complex they haven't gotten right yet.

Session A: they patch. Three correction messages maximum. Best response they can get.

Session B: they write a single restate using everything from the course (role, verb, context, constraint). Send it. First response only.

Compare Session A's best response (after corrections) with Session B's first response (no corrections). Which required more effort? Which response would they actually use?

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The module's exercise asks you to restate and compare, but the comparison is between a restate and a historical memory of a past conversation. Running both live makes the comparison real and simultaneous. The moment when Session B's first response beats Session A's third response — after considerably less effort — is the conversion moment. The effort gap is the insight: not that restating is better, but that it's faster. People change behaviour for efficiency, not for principles.

---

**Preferred pick: Idea 3 (Restate vs Patch Head-to-Head)**

This is the one that changes behaviour. The module text can tell you restating is more efficient — but efficiency is only convincing when you've measured it yourself. Running both sessions simultaneously and comparing the outputs at equal distance from the start produces a real cost comparison. The effort gap is the argument. Idea 1 (the transcript) is excellent for recognition but doesn't produce the personal efficiency discovery. Idea 2 is useful as a diagnostic habit but is backward-looking rather than transformative.

**Concerns:**
Some learners may not have a suitable "real task" to hand when they do this module. A fallback provided task — something concrete and realistic but not too domain-specific — would let everyone complete this exercise even without a personal example. The exercise also requires two separate AI sessions, which slightly raises the friction compared to a single-session exercise.

---

## LOG — E2 — E-04

Strong aha potential, and the module is unusual in that its main argument is pragmatic not technical (restating is faster, not just better). The supplementary content should lean into that efficiency argument rather than the conceptual one. Idea 3 does that most directly. The transcript (Idea 1) is the most emotionally resonant of the three — watching someone spiral when you can see the exit clearly is almost painful — but it's a recognition exercise, not a behaviour change exercise. The length test (Idea 2) is genuinely novel and easy to run, but it's diagnostic rather than transformative. No stretching here — the efficiency angle is the sharpest entry point.

## E-05: Get the Plan First

**Module concept:** For multi-step tasks, asking for the plan before execution costs one message and saves the entire cost of a wrong-direction output.

---

### Idea 1: The Plan Divergence Test

**Format:** Live comparison experiment (two AI sessions)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
Take a real multi-step task. Send it two ways:

Session A (direct): "Write [the thing]." Read the output.

Session B (plan first): "Before you start, tell me how you'd approach [the thing]. Don't do anything yet." Read the plan. Find one thing to adjust. Send the adjusted message.

Then compare the two outputs. But here's the addition: the learner also has to rate both outputs against a simple three-item rubric before looking at the comparison. Rating first prevents post-hoc rationalisation — you commit to your judgment independently before seeing which approach produced it.

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The module's exercise already has this structure. The addition is the pre-commitment rubric. Without it, learners tend to prefer whichever output they saw second (recency bias) or whichever approach felt like more work (sunk cost). The rubric makes the judgment prior to the reveal. The aha becomes: "I rated B higher before I knew which was which. The plan made it better and I can see exactly why."

---

### Idea 2: The Wrong Plan Exercise

**Format:** Provided case with an intentionally bad plan (guided analysis)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
A provided fictional plan — the AI's response to Message 1 for a realistic task (e.g., restructuring an onboarding document). The plan has three steps. One of them is confidently wrong in a subtle way: it proposes to reorganise the document alphabetically instead of by user journey stage. The error is plausible — alphabetical sounds organised — but would produce a genuinely unhelpful output.

The learner's task: read the plan, identify the wrong step, write Message 2 (the redirect). They write their correction before seeing the "correct" version, then compare.

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The module tells you to check each step for errors. This exercise makes catching a subtle plan error feel like the skill it is — not just approval-or-reject but genuine judgment. Many learners will miss the alphabetical-vs-journey error first time, which is exactly the lesson: plans need reading, not rubber-stamping. The moment of "oh, I almost approved something that would have produced the wrong thing" is the aha the module is building toward.

---

### Idea 3: The One-Message-Saved Calculator

**Format:** Retrospective self-analysis worksheet (10 minutes)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
The learner thinks back to a recent multi-step task where they got an output they had to rework significantly. They estimate: how many correction messages did that take? How long did the rework take?

Then they map the task onto the plan-first pattern: what would Message 1 have looked like? What step in the plan would they have caught and corrected? How would Message 2 have been different from the first correction they actually sent?

Final question: "If you had sent Message 1 first, how many correction messages would you have needed?" In most cases: zero or one.

The worksheet ends with a rough time estimate: plan-first cost vs correction cost.

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The efficiency argument is abstract until you apply it to a real task you remember. The calculator makes it concrete: "That restructure took me four correction rounds and about 40 minutes of back-and-forth. A 2-minute plan check would have replaced all of that." Numbers are more convincing than principles. This retrospective analysis also trains learners to identify future candidates for the plan-first pattern — "this task type is always multi-step; I should always plan first."

---

**Preferred pick: Idea 2 (The Wrong Plan Exercise)**

The module's own exercise already covers the live comparison well. What it doesn't do is teach learners how to read a plan critically — the skill of spotting a subtle wrong step before approving it. That's a different skill from knowing to ask for the plan. The Wrong Plan Exercise addresses that gap directly. Catching the alphabetical-vs-journey error is the kind of judgment that only develops through practice, and the provided case lets every learner practice it in the same conditions. The aha — "I nearly rubber-stamped that" — is only available from a case where the wrong answer is plausible, not obvious.

**Concerns:**
The exercise depends on the provided plan containing an error that is genuinely subtle — plausible enough to be missable by a careful reader. This requires careful construction of the case. Too obvious and learners feel patronised. Too subtle and no one catches it. Worth piloting with a few real learners before finalising. Also: the exercise works for the case it presents but learners may not generalise the "read critically" lesson to their own plans. Adding two diagnostic questions ("what made that step look right at first? what would you check for in your own plans?") would help transfer.

---

## LOG — E2 — E-05

The module's core skill is actually two things: (1) knowing to ask for the plan, and (2) knowing how to read the plan critically. The module covers (1) well. The supplementary content should address (2), because that's where the skill actually lives — anyone can ask for the plan, but approving a wrong plan is the failure mode the module doesn't fully address. Idea 2 targets (2) directly. The calculator (Idea 3) is solid for buy-in but doesn't build skill. The live comparison (Idea 1) is good but the module already has it. The critical reading gap is real aha territory.

## E-06: Migration and Re-seed

**Module concept:** A re-seed is not a summary of the old conversation — it is a curated carry-forward of only the load-bearing context, paired with a fresh task statement.

---

### Idea 1: The Over-seeded vs Under-seeded Comparison

**Format:** Live experiment (three short AI sessions)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
Take a real ongoing task or a realistic scenario the learner is comfortable with. Write three versions of a re-seed:

Version A (over-seeded): everything from the previous conversation — the decisions made, the alternatives considered, the drafts that didn't work, the full background.

Version B (under-seeded): just the task. No carry-forward at all.

Version C (right-sized): the three-part re-seed from the module — role + what was decided (one sentence) + new task.

Send all three. Compare the first responses.

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The module explains what a re-seed should and shouldn't contain. This experiment makes the difference between too much and too little concrete and personal. Most learners will be surprised that Version A (the full history) produces a worse response than Version C — the AI doesn't need your journey, it needs your destination. Version B often produces something perfectly serviceable, which generates its own useful insight: the AI's defaults are better than you think, and the re-seed is precision tuning, not mandatory setup.

---

### Idea 2: The Carry-Forward Triage

**Format:** Structured decision worksheet (for use alongside a real AI conversation)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
The learner opens a real conversation that has gone long — more than five messages. For every piece of context that appeared in the conversation, they answer three triage questions:

1. Was this load-bearing in the old conversation?
2. Is it load-bearing for the new task?
3. Can the AI infer it from the task alone?

Only items that answer Yes / Yes / No get into the re-seed.

The worksheet has columns: Context item | Load-bearing (old)? | Load-bearing (new)? | AI can infer? | Include?

After triage, the learner writes the one-sentence carry-forward using only the surviving items.

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The module says "carry forward the output, not the process" and "one sentence." The triage makes that discipline procedural — it turns a vague editorial judgment into a sequence of answerable questions. Most learners will find that 80% of what they thought they needed to carry forward fails the triage. That discovery is the aha: the new conversation needs almost nothing from the old one. The discipline isn't about what to include — it's about what to leave behind.

---

### Idea 3: The Re-seed Quality Ladder

**Format:** Before/after progression (annotated example set)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
Three versions of the same re-seed for the same transition point in a fictional project — weak, medium, and strong. Each is annotated: what's wrong with the weak version (too much process, no clear carry-forward), what the medium version gets right (clear carry-forward, but the task statement is vague), and what makes the strong version work (role + one-sentence decision + full E-03 task structure).

The learner writes their own re-seed for a real transition, then self-rates it against the ladder: which level is mine closest to? What would move it up one level?

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The module provides a template. The ladder shows learners what "wrong" looks like at multiple levels of wrongness — not just absent vs present. Most learners will write a medium re-seed without the ladder; with it, they can see exactly what's missing. The self-rating step forces comparison before the learner can declare the exercise done.

---

**Preferred pick: Idea 1 (The Over-seeded vs Under-seeded Comparison)**

The most counterintuitive finding in this module is that more context doesn't always help — and that the AI's defaults are sometimes better than a full dump of the old conversation. That insight can't be delivered by explanation. It has to be experienced. Sending Version A and Version C and finding that C produces a sharper response is the exact "oh" moment the module is pointing toward. Version B (under-seeded) also produces a useful surprise for many learners. Three sends, genuine comparison, personal stakes.

**Concerns:**
The experiment requires a real scenario the learner is comfortable enough with to write three believable versions of. For learners early in their AI use this may be hard. A provided fictional scenario with the three versions pre-drafted could serve as a fallback, with the learner's own scenario as the "go further" option. Also: the over-seeded version needs to be genuinely over-seeded — if learners write a short Version A, the comparison will be flat. Instructions should encourage them to really load it up.

---

## LOG — E2 — E-06

The conceptual move in this module is less intuitive than it looks. Most people believe "more context is always better" — and the module is arguing something subtle: context quality beats context quantity, and the re-seed is a triage, not a summary. The aha is the discovery that the AI doesn't need your journey — just your destination plus load-bearing facts. Idea 1 delivers that empirically. Ideas 2 and 3 are more analytical/procedural — good tools but they don't produce the same surprise. The over-seeded version doing worse than the right-sized version is where the belief shifts. That's the moment worth engineering.

## E-07: Your Reference Card

**Module concept:** The most useful reference for AI work is a personal one written in your own language for your own most common tasks — built once, used indefinitely.

---

### Idea 1: The Card Stress Test

**Format:** Guided usability exercise (10 minutes, one real task)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
After writing the reference card, the learner faces a deliberately adjacent task — not one of their three templates, but something similar to one of them. A variation they haven't planned for.

They open the card and follow a timer: 90 seconds to adapt a template and send a prompt, without leaving the card to look up the module content.

Then they answer: "Did the card have what you needed? What were you missing? What would you add?"

The timer is the trick. It prevents overthinking and simulates actual use — in practice you won't read the whole card, you'll scan for the part that fits. The 90-second constraint shows whether the card is usable or just complete.

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The module's own exercise asks learners to "use it once" and update if it didn't help. This stress test adds the pressure of time and an unfamiliar-but-adjacent task. Most learners will find one of two problems: the card is too wordy to scan quickly, or the task templates are too specific to adapt. Both are useful findings. The insight is: a reference card that you have to read is not a reference card. It has to be scannable.

---

### Idea 2: The Principles Paraphrase Audit

**Format:** Self-comparison exercise (10 minutes, no AI needed)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
The learner reads their three principles section on the reference card, then closes it and writes them out again from memory in completely different words — no looking.

Then they open the card and compare. Are the memory-versions clearer than the card-versions? If memory produced something better — shorter, more personal — the card-version is too formal. If memory produced something worse — vaguer, inaccurate — the understanding isn't solid yet.

Either way: update the card to match the memory version (or to fix the gap the memory version revealed).

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The module says to write principles "in your own words." Most learners will write close paraphrases of the module's own language, thinking that counts. The memory test bypasses the text entirely — what comes out when the course language is unavailable is what they actually understand. The gap between "my words" and "the course's words I half-remembered" is where the real writing needs to happen. This audit surfaces that gap in a way the writing exercise alone can't.

### Idea 3: The 30-Day Update Protocol

**Format:** Structured habit prompt (a short guide for ongoing use)

**What it contains / what the user does:**
A one-page "how to keep this card working" guide. Every 30 days: one session, three questions.

1. Which template did I use most in the last month?
2. Did anything in the card not work when I tried it — and why?
3. Is there a task I do regularly now that isn't on the card?

For each question that produces an answer: update the card. One change per question maximum.

The guide also includes a "version note" discipline: at the bottom of the card, the learner writes the date of each update and one sentence about what changed. Not for record-keeping — for noticing that the card is alive and evolving, not a fixed artefact.

**Why this creates insight the module text doesn't:**
The module's final line is "A reference card that has been used once is worth ten times a reference card that has only been written." The 30-day protocol operationalises that claim. It prevents the card from becoming an exercise artefact that gets filed and forgotten. The insight it produces isn't intellectual — it's behavioural: the card improves with use, and the people who get the most from it are the people who treat it as a living document. That lesson can't come from reading; it comes from having a protocol that makes the living-document behaviour easy.

---

**Preferred pick: Idea 1 (The Card Stress Test)**

E-07 is the synthesis module — the card is the course's exit artefact. The biggest risk is that learners produce a card that feels complete but isn't usable. The stress test targets usability directly: 90 seconds, adjacent task, no looking at module content. The finding from this test — that the card is too wordy, or too specific, or missing a verb — is more valuable than the card itself, because it produces a second iteration while the learner is still in the course. The other two ideas are good ongoing maintenance habits, but they work after the course. Idea 1 works while the learner can still act on the insight.

**Concerns:**
The 90-second timer could feel arbitrary or stressful to learners with lower confidence. It may be worth framing this as "a quick test, not a race" — the point is to simulate real conditions (brief, pressured, distracted), not to create anxiety. Alternative: offer 2 minutes and emphasise that the time limit is diagnostic, not evaluative. If you can't find what you need in 2 minutes, the card needs editing, not your memory.

## LOG — E2 — E-07

This module is different from the others — it's constructive rather than conceptual, and the output (the reference card) is highly personal. Supplementary content here faces a different challenge: the module text is already doing the right thing by asking learners to build something real. The additions need to test or sharpen that artefact, not build more understanding of the concept. The stress test (Idea 1) tests usability of the card itself. The paraphrase audit (Idea 2) tests depth of understanding. The 30-day protocol (Idea 3) tests durability. Idea 1 wins because it has the tightest feedback loop — the learner sees the problem and can fix it before leaving the course. Ideas 2 and 3 are genuinely useful but both belong in an ongoing practice frame rather than supplementary module content. Slight concern that E-07's synthesis nature means the aha is less conceptual and more "this works for me" — that's a different kind of insight, but it counts.
