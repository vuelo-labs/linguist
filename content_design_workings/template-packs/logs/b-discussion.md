## B-06: Tell It When to Stop

**Strongest idea across both logs:** Both agents picked the stopping conditions reference — B1 as a phrase bank, B2 as a twenty-example cheat sheet organised by type. B2's version is stronger.

Both of us identified the same problem (blank-page lexical failure — learners know they need a stopping condition but cannot produce the language on demand) and both proposed the same category of solution. B2's execution is better: twenty examples organised into four types (item count, word count, format exclusion, combination) is more navigable than B1's three-section phrase bank. The taxonomy gives learners a mental model, not just a list.

**What I am standing firm on:** The phrase bank's exclusion section — "no suggestions I didn't ask for," "no preamble," "no follow-up questions," "no summary at the end." B2's cheat sheet includes these under "format exclusion" but the specific phrasing I used in the phrase bank is slightly more learner-language and worth preserving in the final card copy.

**What I am conceding:** The structural advantage of B2's taxonomy. Four named categories ("by item count / by word count / by format exclusion / by combination") teaches a mental model that a flat list does not. Learners who absorb the taxonomy will eventually generate their own stopping conditions without the card. The flat list does not produce that outcome.

**What B2 said that genuinely changed my thinking:** The combination row in B2's cheat sheet ("3 bullets, each under 15 words, no preamble"). I had not included combinations in my phrase bank. A learner who only ever uses single-condition stops will eventually encounter a response that gets the count right but adds a long introduction anyway. The combination examples teach them that conditions can be stacked, which is a meaningful skill upgrade from just using single conditions.

**One concern to carry forward:** Both agents flagged visual overload as a risk for twenty items on one page. Strong visual category separation is a production requirement, not an option. If design cannot achieve that separation cleanly, reduce to fourteen items (three or four per category) rather than compromise readability.

## Consolidated Reference Card

My answer: yes, build it, but not until the individual module cards exist in final form.

- **B-09:** Your persistent instructions are the things you explain at the start of every conversation. Write them once.

That is nine one-liners that fit on one page. The consolidated reference card is the one-page version of that list — not a replacement for the individual cards, but the "which card do I need?" navigator.
