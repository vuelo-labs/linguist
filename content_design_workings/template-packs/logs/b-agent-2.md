# Agent B2 — B-track Supplementary Content Brainstorm
*Phase 1: Independent brainstorm — Empathy/accessibility lens*
*Date: 2026-04-15*

---

## Framing note

My operating question throughout: what would someone reach for on a bad day, when their confidence is low and the AI just gave them a weird response? That means I'm consistently asking: does this make the learner feel more capable or more confused? Does it require them to already feel okay, or does it work when they're not? Does it stay useful for weeks, or only in the first session?

The through-line: the most useful thing is the thing that is there at the moment of need — not just when someone is in a learning mindset, but when they're mid-task and something has gone sideways.

---

## B-01: Your First Message

**Module concept:** You do not need to know anything about AI — just send one sentence, read the response, and discover that nothing broke.

---

### Idea 1: "What You Just Did" — A Plain-Language Explainer for the Moment After

**Format:** One-page document, printable or saveable

**What it contains:** A short, honest explanation of what actually happened when the learner sent their first message — written for after the exercise, not before it. Sections: "What you actually typed at." (What the AI is, without hype or fear — it predicts useful responses to text.) "Why it answered so fast." "Why the wording is slightly different every time." "Whether it recorded your message or told anyone." (No, with the one real caveat about free plans.) "What it cannot do." (Not a comprehensive list — just the two or three that matter most for beginners: it cannot know current events, cannot remember last time, cannot see outside the conversation.)

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** The first message is sent successfully, but many learners still feel shaky. They sent the message, the AI responded, but they don't really know what happened. Understanding — even superficially — removes the feeling that they are using something they fundamentally don't understand. The concept (nothing broke, AI responded, this is how it works) is explained *after* the experience, which means it lands differently than a pre-explanation would.

**Why returnable:** When someone encounters something odd in week 3 — a very long response, a weird phrasing — this is the document they return to. "Oh, it's just doing what it always does."

---

### Idea 2: "Ten Things That Are Not Problems" — A Pre-Send Confidence Card

**Format:** Credit-card-sized digital card or printable strip

**What it contains:** A numbered list of ten things learners fear that are not actually problems: (1) Asking a question that sounds stupid. (2) Getting a response that's too long. (3) Ignoring a response and starting again. (4) Sending the exact same message twice. (5) Not saying please or thank you. (6) Writing in ALL CAPS or terrible punctuation. (7) Closing the tab mid-conversation. (8) Not reading the whole response. (9) Asking for something and deciding you didn't need it. (10) Getting something totally wrong and having to start over. No explanation for any of them — just the list.

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** For a genuinely anxious beginner, the obstacle to sending the first message is not knowledge — it is the feeling that they might transgress some rule they don't know about. This card names the ten most common phantom rules and cancels them. The concept (nothing broke) is reinforced before the message is even sent. The format is deliberately minimal: a list, no prose, nothing to read carefully, just enough to scan and feel reassured.

**Why returnable:** Pin it above the keyboard. Reach for it every time you feel the hesitation before pressing Send.

---

### Idea 3: "Your First Week With AI" — A Loose Daily Prompt Schedule

**Format:** One-page printable, seven boxes

**What it contains:** Seven very short prompts — one per day — designed to ease someone into using AI over their first week. Day 1: the B-01 exercise (already provided in the module). Day 2: Ask the AI something you've been curious about but haven't looked up. Day 3: Use it for something practical — a shopping list, a to-do list, a recipe idea. Day 4: Try a second version of something you did last week. Day 5: Ask it to help you write one sentence (just one). Day 6: Send something you've been putting off. Day 7: Send the same prompt you used on Day 1. See if you feel different about sending it this time.

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** The most common reason learners stop after one module is they don't have a reason to return. This gives them a reason — a very small, low-stakes one — every day for a week. The concept reinforced is: AI is just a thing you use regularly, not a system you have to master before you can start. By Day 7, the first-message anxiety is structurally impossible — they've already sent six messages.

**Anxious-user note:** The daily prompts need to feel optional, not like a schedule they can fail. "You don't have to do these in order. Pick the one that sounds easiest today." 

---

### Preferred pick: Idea 2 — "Ten Things That Are Not Problems" card

**Reasoning:** B-01's job is to get someone through the door. The biggest barrier for an anxious user is not missing knowledge — it is the feeling that there are rules they might break. The card directly addresses that by explicitly listing the non-rules. It requires the smallest possible effort to use (scan, not read), it fits in a pocket, and it works every time they feel the pre-send hesitation, not just on day one. The post-send explainer (Idea 1) is good for understanding, but understanding is not what stops people from pressing Send — fear is. The weekly schedule (Idea 3) is genuinely helpful for retention but operates over a longer time horizon than the acute confidence problem B-01 is addressing.

**Concern:** Ten is a lot for a card. The items need to be ordered from most feared to least, so someone who only reads the first three still gets the most important reassurance. Also: this card has no return value after the first week or so — it is useful during the initial anxiety phase only. That's fine; it earns its place anyway.

## B-02: Copy, Personalise, Use

**Module concept:** Every template in this course has square brackets — replace them with your own details, one at a time, and the message becomes yours.

---

### Idea 1: "Bracket Decoder" — A Translator Card for the Six Most Common Bracket Types

**Format:** A6 card, printable or digital, double-sided if needed

**What it contains:** Six bracket types, each defined in one sentence with two concrete examples from very different everyday contexts (not just "professional" examples):

- **[your role]** — your job, or your main role right now. E.g. "secondary school teacher" / "parent of two kids"
- **[task]** — what you actually need done. E.g. "summarising a meeting" / "planning a birthday party"
- **[context]** — one fact that makes your situation specific. E.g. "the audience has no technical knowledge" / "this is for a child, not an adult"
- **[length]** — how much you want back. E.g. "3 bullet points" / "under 100 words" / "one short paragraph"
- **[tone]** — how it should sound. E.g. "casual and warm" / "professional but not stiff"
- **[what to leave out]** — one thing you don't want. E.g. "no jargon" / "don't suggest things I didn't ask for"

Footer: "You don't need to be exact. Put in your best guess and send it."

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** The Copy → Personalise → Use pattern is conceptually simple, but freezing in front of an unfamiliar bracket is a real failure mode. This card makes every bracket answerable in under ten seconds. The confidence benefit is: there is no bracket type that doesn't have an obvious answer waiting for the learner. The concept is reinforced by naming the categories — over time, learners stop needing the card because they've absorbed the types.

---

### Idea 2: "Good Enough Is Good" — A Gallery of Imperfect But Working Examples

**Format:** Short document, three before/after pairs

**What it contains:** Three bracket templates, each shown filled in by a fictional ordinary person who is not particularly articulate — their personalisation is slightly awkward, slightly vague, but it works. The filled-in examples are deliberately imperfect. Alongside each: the AI response it would produce (described in one sentence) and a note: "This is not polished. It doesn't need to be. It was specific enough to get a useful response." Scenarios: someone filling in a bracket with a rough job title they're not confident about; someone using "I'm not sure but maybe..." in the context slot; someone leaving a bracket slightly too general but close enough.

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** The anxiety in B-02 is often perfectionism — learners worry their personalisation isn't good enough to work. Seeing intentionally imperfect examples that still produce useful results directly addresses this. The concept reinforced is: the bar is lower than you think. If it is close to your situation, it will work.

**Anxious-user note:** This idea risks being patronising if the "imperfect" examples are too obviously imperfect. They need to feel like real things a real person would write — slightly hesitant, not comically wrong. "I'm sort of a project coordinator, I think" is the right register.

---

### Idea 3: "Your Most Common Task" — A One-Time Setup Sheet

**Format:** Single page, structured prompt

**What it contains:** A guided one-time setup exercise: "Think of the thing you most often need to write or produce at work (or in life). Answer these five questions: What is it? Who reads it? How long should it be? What tone does it need? What do you never want it to include?" At the bottom: "Now fill in this template with your answers: I'm a [your role] working on [your task]. I need you to [verb] this. [Your length]. [Your tone]. Don't include [what to leave out]." A note: "Save this. This is your most-used personalised template. Any time you need [that thing], paste this in and replace the content."

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** Completing this exercise gives the learner a template that is already theirs — they filled it in themselves, it reflects their actual work. The concept reinforced is: personalisation is a one-time investment that keeps paying out. The confidence benefit is ownership: they have a ready-made message for their most common task.

---

### Preferred pick: Idea 1 — Bracket Decoder card

**Reasoning:** The card serves the learner at the exact moment of friction: cursor blinking, template open, unsure what to put in [context]. It answers that question in under ten seconds, without requiring any prior reading. Idea 2 (imperfect examples gallery) is genuinely valuable for reducing the perfectionism barrier, but it requires more reading and works better as a module supplement than a standalone card. Idea 3 (one-time setup sheet) is the most practically useful of the three for daily use, but it requires effort at a point in the course when the learner may not yet trust the system enough to invest time in setup. The decoder card earns its place because it can be used immediately and requires no commitment.

**Concern:** The non-professional examples (e.g. "parent of two kids" as a role) may confuse learners who are using this course for work purposes. The card needs to make clear that [your role] means whatever is most relevant to the task at hand, not necessarily a job title.

## B-03: Platform Orientation

**Module concept:** A reference module for where things live on your specific platform — come back to it whenever you can't find something.

---

### Idea 1: "My Platform" — A Single-Platform Quick Reference Card (three versions: Claude / ChatGPT / Gemini)

**Format:** Three cards, each A6, learner keeps only the one for their platform

**What it contains:** For one platform per card: where to type, how to start a new chat, where history lives, where settings are, where persistent instructions are set up (Project / Custom Instructions / Gem), what to do if you can't find something ("search help, or ask the AI itself: 'Where do I find [thing] in [platform name]?'"). Written in spatial language only — "left sidebar," "bottom of the screen," "top-left corner" — not in button names that change with updates. Each card is headed: "You are using [Platform name]. Keep this card. Everything you need is on it."

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** B-03 is a reference module — the supplementary content should be an even more returnable version of that reference. The card format strips out the other two platforms the learner is not using, which has a small but meaningful effect on confidence: it signals "you only need to know about this one thing." The concept (where things live) is reinforced by physical handling of the card that covers their platform only.

---

### Idea 2: "The Four Things You'll Always Need to Find" — A Platform-Agnostic Checklist

**Format:** Small card

**What it contains:** Four items, described function-first, not name-first:
1. "Where you type your message" — the input box
2. "How to start a completely fresh conversation" — New chat / New conversation
3. "Where your old conversations are saved" — History / Sidebar
4. "Where to find persistent instructions" — Platform-specific name

For each: one sentence on why it matters. "Starting a fresh conversation is important when a current one has gone off track (B-08). Knowing how to do it quickly removes a lot of anxiety about making mistakes."

At the bottom: "These are the only four features you need to know for all the exercises in this course. Everything else is optional."

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** Naming only four things sends a clear signal: this is not a system to master, it is a tool with a small number of features that matter. The concept reinforced is: platform orientation is not about knowing everything, it is about knowing where four things are. The confidence benefit is scope-setting — the learner now knows the list is short.

---

### Idea 3: "Ask the Platform, or Ask the AI" — A Two-Path Recovery Guide

**Format:** A5 card

**What it contains:** A short guide for what to do when you can't find something on the platform. Two paths:

**Path 1 — Ask the platform:** "Most platforms have a Help menu or a search function. Type what you're looking for. 'How do I start a new conversation.' 'Where are my settings.' These usually work."

**Path 2 — Ask the AI:** "The AI knows its own platform reasonably well. Open a new chat and type: 'I'm using [platform]. Where do I find [the thing I'm looking for]?' It will give you step-by-step directions."

Plus: "If neither works, check the platform's name in the B-03 module on your course — the reference section for your platform will have the answer."

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** Platform confusion is a confidence killer in a very specific way: it makes the learner feel stupid about technology, which is exactly what this course is trying to undo. Knowing there are two independent ways to find what you need — and that asking the AI is one of them — gives learners a genuinely useful tool. The concept reinforced is: you don't need to know where everything is before you start; you can find anything when you need it.

---

### Preferred pick: Idea 1 — Per-platform one-card reference (three versions)

**Reasoning:** B-03 is explicitly a reference module. The supplementary content's job is to be the most returnable form of that reference. A card that covers only the learner's platform — with the learner's platform name at the top — is the cleanest possible version of this. Idea 2 (four things you need) is a stronger confidence-builder in tone, but requires the learner to map "left sidebar" to their actual platform without the card telling them which platform they're on. Idea 3 (recovery paths) is excellent as a supplementary idea and could work well alongside Idea 1, but it doesn't replace the core reference function. The platform-specific card wins on relevance and return-ability.

**Concern:** Platform UIs change. The card needs a version date and a note: "UI details may vary after [date]. If something looks different, the B-03 module has the latest reference." The card is most useful as a printed object; for digital users it needs to be a pinnable note, not a PDF they can't find.

## B-04: Your Input Shapes the Output

**Module concept:** The detail you put into a message determines the quality of what comes back — you can control this deliberately.

---

### Idea 1: "Before You Send" — A Four-Question Pre-Send Mental Check

**Format:** Small card or sticky note (designed to sit next to a keyboard)

**What it contains:** Four questions, one per line:

1. Have I said who I am?
2. Have I said exactly what I need?
3. Have I said what matters about my situation?
4. Have I said what "done" looks like?

Below the questions: "If you answered yes to all four, this message has what it needs. If you answered no to any one, add one sentence for that gap."

Footer: "You do not need to answer all four perfectly. Just check them."

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** The module's core insight is that input quality shapes output quality. But "add more detail" is abstract advice. Four concrete questions make it actionable in 15 seconds. The confidence benefit is: the learner always has a path forward when a message feels thin. They don't have to guess what to add — the card tells them which slot is empty.

**Why returnable:** This card earns its place every single session, not just while learning B-04. It is the most habitual object in the whole pack.

---

### Idea 2: "Same Task, Different Input" — A Personal Comparison Log

**Format:** Single-page double-entry log, printable

**What it contains:** Structured space for the learner to run the module's experiment and record what they found. Four columns: Task, Short message sent, Detailed message sent, What was different about the responses. Space for two rounds — one from the module exercise, one of their own choosing. Below the log: "Look at the difference between your two responses. Write one sentence: 'When I added detail about [X], the AI's response [was more specific / was more useful / was the right length].'" This sentence is their personal evidence that the concept works.

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** Writing "when I added [X], the AI did [Y]" in their own words is a different kind of learning than reading it in the module. It produces a piece of evidence the learner owns. If they doubt the concept later, they have their own log. The concept reinforced is: this is not a rule, it is a mechanism you personally verified.

**Anxious-user note:** The log format is light enough that it doesn't feel like homework — two runs, one sentence conclusion. It should be framed: "This is for you, not for anyone else. You don't have to show anyone."

---

### Idea 3: "The Three Things That Always Help" — A Pocket Cheat Sheet for Adding Detail

**Format:** Half an A5 page or small card

**What it contains:** Three things to add to any message that reliably improve the response:

1. **Your situation in one sentence.** "I'm a [your role] working on [what you're doing]." — This tells the AI who you are and what context you're in.
2. **A specific angle.** "Focus on [the part that matters most to you]." — This tells the AI what you actually care about, not just what you're asking about.
3. **A done condition.** "Keep it to [format and length]." — This tells the AI when to stop.

Below: "You don't need all three every time. One of these added to a short message will improve the response. All three will usually give you exactly what you needed."

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** The module compares a short and detailed version, but doesn't give learners a quick formula for turning a short message into a detailed one. This card does. The concept (your input shapes the output) is made actionable in three steps that anyone can apply to any message. The confidence benefit is: there is always something you can do to improve a message. You are never stuck with "I don't know what to add."

---

### Preferred pick: Idea 1 — "Before You Send" pre-send check

**Reasoning:** B-04's core insight is easy to understand but easy to forget in the moment of use. The pre-send check is a habit-forming tool that sits at the point of action — right before the learner presses Send. Idea 3 (three things that help) is more instructional — it tells the learner what to add. The check card takes one step back and asks whether those things are there. The difference is subtle but matters: the check card prompts independent thinking ("have I said who I am?"), while Idea 3 prescribes three additions. For an anxious learner, the prescriptive card is probably easier to use initially. But I prefer the check because it is generative rather than formulaic — learners become better at knowing what to add, not just better at following the three-step formula. The comparison log (Idea 2) is the strongest learning tool of the three but is most useful as a one-time exercise. It doesn't have the return value of the check card.

**Concern:** The check card needs to be short enough to scan in 10 seconds. Four questions at the current length are fine. If any language gets more complicated, it will not be used.

## B-05: One Thing at a Time

**Module concept:** Bundling three tasks in one message usually produces three mediocre results; sending them one at a time lets you check each step before moving to the next.

---

### Idea 1: "The 'And' Test" — A Single-Rule Pre-Send Card

**Format:** Credit-card-sized card

**What it contains:** One rule on the front, one example on the back.

**Front:** "Before you send: does your message contain 'and'? If yes, consider whether you have two tasks."

**Back:** "Example: 'Summarise this article **and** write a social media post about it' = two tasks. Split them. Send the summary first. Check it. Then ask for the post."

Footer: "Exception: 'and' in the middle of one task is fine. 'Draft a short email that is warm and professional' is one task."

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** "And" is the clearest signal of task bundling. This rule is simple enough to remember, specific enough to apply, and forgiving enough (the exception) not to make learners paranoid about using the word "and." The concept (one thing per message) is reduced to a one-second check. The confidence benefit is: there is a concrete, learnable test — not an abstract principle.

---

### Idea 2: "What I'm Waiting For Before I Send the Next Message" — A Three-Column Review Sheet

**Format:** Single-page printable, three columns

**What it contains:** Three columns for any multi-step task: "What I sent," "What I got back," "What I need to check before moving on." Space for three rounds per task. A prompt above each "What I need to check" column: "Was it accurate? Did it cover the right things? Would I use it as-is, or do I need one change first?" Footer: "When you can answer 'yes, I'd use this as-is' — then send the next message."

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** The module's key insight is that you should check each response before moving on. This sheet formalises the checking step as something the learner does, not just something the module says is a good idea. The concept (one task, check, next task) is given a physical structure. The confidence benefit is: the learner always knows where they are in the process.

**Anxious-user note:** The sheet should feel like a scratchpad, not a worksheet. It's messy by design — not every field needs to be filled in every time.

---

### Idea 3: "Split It First" — A Prompt Decomposer Card

**Format:** A6 card

**What it contains:** A quick-look guide for the most common multi-task bundles, each split into numbered steps:

- "Summarise this + write a social post about it" → (1) Summarise in 5 bullets. (2) Using that summary, write a 280-character post.
- "Draft an email + suggest a subject line" → (1) Draft the email. (2) Suggest 3 subject line options for what you just drafted.
- "Explain this + tell me what to do next" → (1) Explain this in plain language. (2) Based on that, what are my next three options?
- "Write a first draft + improve it" → (1) Draft a first version. (2) Review it for [tone / length / clarity] and rewrite.

Footer: "You don't need all of these memorised. Just use this to split your task before you start typing."

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** Seeing the split-and-sequence work already done — for the exact types of multi-task bundles learners actually send — removes the effort of figuring out the sequence yourself. The concept is reinforced through five concrete examples of the same principle applied differently. The confidence benefit is: you don't have to invent the split, just recognise your task in the list.

---

### Preferred pick: Idea 1 — "The 'And' Test" card

**Reasoning:** B-05 is a module where the concept is absorbed quickly and the habit takes longer to form. The 'And' Test card addresses the habit gap: it provides a concrete check that takes one second and works in any context. The decomposer card (Idea 3) is the most practical reference — it shows common splits pre-done. But it only helps if your task matches one of the four examples. The 'And' test works for any task. The review sheet (Idea 2) does good work for complex multi-step tasks but requires the learner to already be in the right mode — it doesn't interrupt the habit of bundling, it just structures what happens after you've already decided to split. The 'And' test interrupts the habit at the right moment: before the Send button.

**Concern:** The exception note on the back is necessary but adds complexity. If it needs to be there, it is evidence that the rule has an edge case that could confuse people. The framing should be: "If you have two verbs, you have two tasks" — which is cleaner than the 'and' test and doesn't need an exception.

## B-06: Tell It When to Stop

**Module concept:** Without a stopping condition, the AI guesses how much to say and usually says too much — one line at the end of your message fixes this.

---

### Idea 1: "Stopping Condition Formats" — A 20-Example Reference Organised by Type

**Format:** Single-page cheat sheet, four categories

**What it contains:** Twenty ready-to-use stopping conditions, sorted:

**By item count:** "3 bullet points." / "5 options." / "4 steps." / "One list only."

**By word count:** "Under 80 words." / "No more than one paragraph." / "Under 150 words." / "One sentence per point."

**By format exclusion:** "No introduction." / "No conclusion." / "No preamble." / "Just the list." / "No suggestions I didn't ask for."

**By combination:** "3 bullets, each under 15 words, no preamble." / "Under 100 words, no introduction." / "5 steps, each under 20 words, numbered."

Footer: "You can combine any of these. Pick one from any category, or mix two."

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** The concept is easy; the execution failure is blank-page: someone knows they want to constrain the response but can't phrase it on the spot. This sheet takes that problem away entirely. The concept is reinforced through the taxonomy: learners begin to see that every stopping condition is one of four types, which helps them generate their own in future without the sheet.

**Why this is the most returnable item in the whole pack:** Reached for every session, not just while learning B-06. It is a working tool.

---

### Idea 2: "Add This Line" — A Suffix Prompt Strip to Slide Into Any Message

**Format:** Small strip (physical or digital sticky note), seven options

**What it contains:** Seven stopping condition phrases, each written as a complete sentence suffix — designed to be added to the end of any message with zero editing:

- "Stop after 3 bullet points."
- "Keep it under 80 words."
- "No introduction, no conclusion."
- "One paragraph only."
- "Give me 5 options, each in one sentence."
- "No preamble — just the [summary / list / draft / steps]."
- "Exactly 3 steps. No more."

Plus one blank line: "My own: _______________" — so the learner can add one they commonly use.

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** The suffix framing is practical: these are not instructions to read, they are sentences to copy. The blank line matters: filling it in once (after encountering the right stopping condition for their common task) produces a tool they own. The concept reinforced is: a stopping condition is a line you add, not a skill you perform.

### Idea 3: "What Happens Without One" — Three Short Scenario Cards

**Format:** Three short vignettes, each half a page, read once

**What it contains:** Three brief, recognisable scenarios from everyday use where an absent stopping condition causes a familiar frustration:

1. "I asked for advice on a decision and got a 600-word essay when I needed three options." → What would have fixed it: "Give me 3 options. One sentence each."
2. "I asked for help drafting an email and got five versions plus a commentary on each." → What would have fixed it: "Draft one version. Under 100 words."
3. "I asked it to explain something and got its entire history when I needed one sentence." → What would have fixed it: "Explain in one sentence. No context or background."

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** The experience of being annoyed by an overly long AI response is nearly universal for beginners. These vignettes name that experience and resolve it, which does two things: makes the learner feel understood, and gives them the specific stopping condition they needed. The concept is reinforced through recognition: oh, that's what happened to me. And this is how to fix it.

**Anxious-user note:** The most important thing about these vignettes is that the frustration is framed as normal, not as the learner's error. "This is what happens when you don't include one. It's not your fault — the AI defaults to thoroughness when it doesn't have guidance."

### Preferred pick: Idea 1 — Stopping Conditions cheat sheet

**Reasoning:** B-06 introduces a concept that learners understand immediately but apply inconsistently — not because they forget, but because they can't produce the right phrasing on demand. The cheat sheet eliminates that blank-page problem. It is also the most frequently returned-to item in the pack: stopping conditions are needed in every AI session, not just during B-06. The suffix strip (Idea 2) is more actionable (copy-paste ready), but the cheat sheet's advantage is its organisation — seeing the categories helps learners develop their own variants. The vignettes (Idea 3) are the best at building understanding and recognition, but understanding is not the gap; phrasing is. The cheat sheet serves the gap.

**Concern:** Twenty items on one page risks visual overload. The categories need strong visual separation (headers, whitespace, possibly colour if the format allows) so the learner can scan to the right category in under five seconds. If it looks dense, it will not be used.

## B-07: What Just Happened? Reading AI Output

**Module concept:** The AI's first response is a draft, not a final answer — reading it critically before reacting is the core skill.

---

### Idea 1: "Four Questions" — A Laminated Output Checklist Card

**Format:** Credit-card to A6 size, laminated or pinned digitally

**What it contains:** The four checklist questions from the module in their most portable form:

1. Did it answer what I actually asked?
2. Is anything generic where it should be specific?
3. Did it invent anything I didn't give it?
4. Is the length about right?

Below each question: one signal phrase that indicates a "no." (1) "Could this apply to anyone, or does it mention my situation?" (2) "Would this response be the same if I hadn't said who I am?" (3) "Are there names, statistics, or quotes I didn't provide?" (4) "Did I ask for 3 bullets and get 12 paragraphs?"

Bottom: a verdict strip — "Use it / Change one thing / Start over."

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** The module provides this checklist; this is that checklist in its most returnable form. The signal phrases are the key value-add — they give the learner a concrete trigger for recognising a "no" answer. The verdict strip converts the checklist from a reading tool into a decision tool. The concept (read before you react) becomes a physical ritual.

**Why this is the most important card in the whole pack:** When the AI gives a weird response and the learner doesn't know what to do — this is what they reach for. It tells them what to check and what to do next. It is the compass for every session from B-07 onward.

### Idea 2: "What Changed When I Read It" — A Post-Response Reflection Slip

**Format:** Single slip of paper (or digital note), very short

**What it contains:** Four fill-in-the-blank lines for after the learner reads a response:

- "The response answered / didn't answer what I asked because ___."
- "The most useful part was ___."
- "The part that was off was ___."
- "What I'll do next: use it / ask for one change / start over."

At the top: "Fill this in after any response that surprised you. You don't need to fill it in when things work fine."

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** Writing down observations about a response — even one sentence each — builds the evaluative muscle the module is teaching. The "what I'll do next" line is important: it makes the decision explicit rather than leaving the learner stuck in "what do I do now." The confidence benefit is: they always have a path forward, even when the response was strange.

**Anxious-user note:** This slip needs to be brief enough to fill in during the session, not after. If it takes longer than 30 seconds, it becomes an optional activity learners skip. Four very short lines is the right length.

### Idea 3: "It's Allowed to Be Wrong" — A Permissions Card for Critical Reading

**Format:** Small card, less is more

**What it contains:** Six explicit permissions:

- "You are allowed to use only part of the response."
- "You are allowed to check facts the AI stated as true."

Footer: "The AI does not have feelings about being asked to redo something. It has no preference."

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** The module teaches evaluation — but many anxious learners are reluctant to push back on the AI because they feel like they should use whatever it gave them, or that asking for changes is impolite or wasteful. This card names the permissions explicitly. The concept (the first response is a draft, not a final answer) is converted into six specific actions the learner is allowed to take.

**Anxious-user note:** "The AI does not have feelings about being asked to redo something" is the single most useful sentence in the whole pack for a certain type of learner — the one who feels bad about asking for a change. It needs to be on this card.

**Reasoning:** The module explicitly provides this checklist and says to keep it. This supplementary content item makes good on that intention by giving it a physical, returnable form. ## B-08: Recovery — When It Does Not Work

**Module concept:** When a conversation goes badly off track, the fix is not more corrections — it is a restate in the same conversation, or a completely fresh start.

---

### Idea 1: "Which Move?" — A Recovery Decision Strip

**Format:** Small strip, three rows

**What it contains:** A text-based decision sequence for the exact moment something has gone wrong:

**Row 1 — One detail is wrong:** "Ask for that specific change. Stay in the same conversation. Be precise about what to change and what to keep."

**Row 2 — The AI understood the wrong task:** "Write a fresh first message in the same conversation. Use the restate template. Start the message with: 'I need to start this over.'"

**Row 3 — You've tried twice and it keeps going wrong:** "Open a new conversation. Bring only the core task. Everything else starts fresh."

At the bottom: "Not sure which row you're in? Ask yourself: was it working on the right task, or the wrong one entirely?"

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** The three recovery moves in the module can feel abstract. This strip maps them to the real experience — the learner knows which row they're in based on what they're feeling, not what they understand about the theory. The confidence benefit is: there is always a next step. You are never just stuck.

---

### Idea 2: "What To Bring Forward" — A New-Chat Prep Card

**Format:** Small card

**What it contains:** A guide for the one thing that makes a fresh-start conversation better than the one it's replacing: being intentional about what you carry forward. Four prompts:

1. "What was the task, in one sentence?"
2. "What did the AI get wrong in the previous conversation?"
3. "What detail was missing from your original message?"
4. "What does a good response look like?"

A note: "Start your new conversation with a message that answers these four questions. You don't need to explain what went wrong — just tell the AI what you need, more clearly than last time."

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** Starting a new conversation is the right move when a thread has gone badly — but many learners feel defeated rather than empowered when they do it. They feel like they are undoing their work. This card reframes the fresh start as a sharper attempt, not a failure. The concept reinforced is: a new conversation with a better first message will usually outperform three rounds of corrections in a broken one.

---

### Idea 3: "When It Goes Wrong, It's Not You" — A Normalising One-Pager

**Format:** Single page, five scenarios and fixes

**What it contains:** Five common failure scenarios, each with a plain-language "what happened" explanation and a one-sentence fix:

- "The response was completely off-topic." → The first message was ambiguous. Restate with a clearer task and context.
- "It answered a different question than I asked." → The task wasn't specific enough. Add a verb and a specific object.
- "Each response gets worse after I correct it." → The conversation has accumulated conflicting signals. Start a new conversation.
- "It gave me something technically correct but completely useless." → The done condition was missing. Next time: specify format, length, and what to leave out.
- "It made up a specific fact I know isn't true." → Don't correct it in that conversation — the error will recur. Start fresh and add: 'Only use information I give you.'

**Why it builds confidence AND reinforces the concept:** The normalising title matters. "When it goes wrong, it's not you" is a relief for learners who assume that AI failures are their fault — usually because they don't know enough. The one-page format addresses multiple scenarios without requiring the learner to figure out which one applies; they skim until they recognise their situation. The concept reinforced is: every failure has a known cause and a simple fix.

---

### Preferred pick: Idea 1 — Recovery Decision Strip

**Reasoning:** B-08 is the emotionally hardest module — it is explicitly about things going wrong. The decision strip's job is to interrupt the spiral of "I've tried three times and it keeps getting worse" with a clear next action. The three-row structure maps to the three levels of wrong: one thing off, whole task off, conversation broken. A learner who is frustrated doesn't need to understand why the three levels exist — they just need to know which row they're in and what to do next. Idea 3 (normalising one-pager) is genuinely valuable and addresses the emotional dimension more directly. I'd want both in the pack. Idea 2 (what to bring forward) is excellent as a complement to the fresh start move — but only useful after the learner has already decided to start fresh.

**Concern:** The strip works best if it is physically small — three rows, large enough to read, small enough to be glanced at rather than read. If it grows into a page, it stops being a quick-reference and becomes something the learner has to engage with at length, which is the wrong mode for the moment of frustration.

## Overall pack summary — accessibility and empathy lens

### What the nervous-user lens produced across all ten modules:

**Pattern 1: Cards beat pages.** When something competes between a one-card reference and a one-page document, the card wins for a learner with low confidence. The page is for learning; the card is for use. Both are needed; the card earns its place as the pick.

**Pattern 2: Permissions before instructions.** The permissions card for B-07 ("It's allowed to be wrong") is the best example of this: before teaching a learner what to do, sometimes you have to tell them what they're allowed to do. Anxious learners often already know the skill; they just don't believe they're permitted to use it.

**Pattern 3: Name the fear, don't manage it.** "Ten things that are not problems" (B-01) and "when it goes wrong, it's not you" (B-08) are more effective than reassurance that avoids naming what the learner is afraid of. The confidence comes from recognition: oh, that's what I was worried about — and apparently it's fine.

**Pattern 4: Return value over exercise value.** Every time I had to choose between an exercise (valuable once) and a reference (valuable every time), the reference won. The B-track learner needs tools they reach for on day 14, not just day 1.

**Pattern 5: Pre-filled beats blank.** The "My role is always: ___" line on the Opening Seed card, and the version date on the configuration sheet, are small additions that produce meaningfully more personal artifacts. The learner who fills in one line on a card owns that card in a way they don't if every line is blank.

### The three items I'd most want to exist:

1. **"Four Questions" checklist card (B-07)** — the compass for every AI session from module 7 onward. The learner who picks this up on a bad day knows exactly what to check.
2. **Recovery Decision Strip (B-08)** — the card for the worst moment: three tries and still broken. Three rows. One is where they are.
3. **Stopping Conditions Cheat Sheet (B-06)** — reached for in every single session. Not a learning tool; a working tool.

### The item I'm most uncertain about:

**"The 'And' Test" card (B-05).** A one-rule card is either the most elegant thing in the pack or the most forgettable. It needs to be physically placed somewhere the learner types — desk, pinned note, desktop wallpaper — or it will never be used. The format works only if the placement problem is solved.

### The gap I notice across all ten modules:

Nothing in the pack directly addresses the learner who feels like they're falling behind, or who comes back after two weeks away and feels like they've forgotten everything. A short re-entry card — "You haven't forgotten. Here are the three things that cover 80% of what you need. Start here." — might be the most valuable item not currently proposed. It belongs as a bonus beyond the module-level picks.
