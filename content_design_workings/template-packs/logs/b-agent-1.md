## B-01: Your First Message

**Module concept in one sentence:** Send one message to an AI, read the response, and discover that nothing broke and no prior knowledge was required.

---

### Supplementary content ideas

**Idea 1: "Nothing Broke" Confidence Log**
- Format: Printable one-page worksheet
- Contents / what the user does: A single-page sheet with three rows. Each row has: a box to write the message they sent, a box to write what the AI said back in their own words, and a checkbox labelled "Nothing broke." The third row also has a prompt: "What surprised you?" The user fills this in for their first three AI messages ever — not necessarily in one sitting. The sheet is designed to be kept.
- Why it reinforces: The module's goal is not skill — it is proof that the experience is survivable. The log externalises that proof. When a learner feels anxious again later, they can look at their own completed sheet and see that it worked three times before.

**Idea 2: Five Starter Messages (Personalised)**
- Format: Prompt template pack — a short PDF or printable card with 5 ready-to-send messages
- Contents / what the user does: Five low-stakes messages across five different categories (something curious, something practical, something creative, something about their job, something silly). Each has one [bracket] to fill in so it feels personal. Examples: "What are three ways to make [a task I hate at work] less tedious?" and "What would a professional chef say about [a dish I cooked recently]?" The user picks whichever one feels least scary and sends it.
- Why it reinforces: The module gives one starter message. Some learners need to see more options before they feel safe enough to pick one. Having five reduces the paralysis of "but I don't know what to say."

**Idea 3: "What AI Did vs What I Expected" Comparison Card**
- Format: Two-column fill-in card (printed or in a notes app)
- Contents / what the user does: Left column: "Before I sent it, I thought the AI would..." Right column: "What it actually did..." Three rows. The user fills this in after their first session. At the bottom, a prompt: "Was the reality better, worse, or just different?" Space for one sentence.
- Why it reinforces: Anxiety before first contact is almost always driven by a specific worst-case expectation. The card makes that expectation explicit, then confronts it with reality. The act of writing "I thought it would judge me / I thought it would break / I thought it would charge me" and then "it just answered" is more powerful than any reassurance a course can give.

**Preferred pick: Idea 3 — "What AI Did vs What I Expected" Comparison Card**

This one is the most honest about what is actually happening emotionally in B-01. The module says "nothing broke" — but the real work is defusing whatever the learner feared would break. A card that surfaces the fear and then shows the reality is the one they would keep. It is also genuinely quick to make and use. Ideas 1 and 2 are useful, but Idea 1 feels more like a habit-tracker than a learning tool, and Idea 2 is already partially covered by the module's own Step 2 exercise.

**Concerns:** The comparison card only works if the learner actually had a fear to surface. Some learners will be curious rather than anxious and will find the card a bit therapy-adjacent in a way that feels odd. It might need a lighter framing — "What did you expect vs what actually happened?" rather than leaning into the word anxiety.

## B-02: Copy, Personalise, Use

**Module concept in one sentence:** Take any template with [square bracket placeholders], replace every bracket with your own real words, and send something that is actually yours.

---

### Supplementary content ideas

**Idea 1: "Fill In the Blanks" Starter Pack — 10 Templates Across Real Situations**
- Format: Prompt template pack — printable or shareable PDF, one page, 10 templates
- Contents / what the user does: Ten personalise-and-send templates covering situations the target audience actually faces: writing an apology email, preparing for a difficult conversation, summarising a long document, asking for feedback, planning a difficult week, explaining something technical in plain language, drafting a polite refusal, writing a birthday message that sounds personal, asking for a recommendation letter, preparing for an interview. Each template has [brackets] already in place. The user picks one they need this week, fills in the brackets, sends it.
- Why it reinforces: B-02 teaches the Copy → Personalise → Use pattern using one template. Learners need to see that pattern applied to situations they actually face before it becomes a habit. Ten templates makes it clear the pattern is general, not just for the one example in the lesson.

**Idea 2: "Bracket Spotting" Practice Worksheet**
- Format: Worksheet — two or three worked examples with wrong and right personalisations side by side
- Contents / what the user does: Three short template messages. For each one, the worksheet shows two filled-in versions: one where the brackets were left vague or half-filled ("I'm a [worker] working on [a project]") and one where they were filled in specifically ("I'm a part-time bookkeeper working on reconciling last month's bank statement"). The learner reads both responses from the AI side by side — shown on the worksheet — and identifies which produced a better result and why. A short reflection prompt at the bottom.
- Why it reinforces: The module warns "if it sounds like something you would actually say about your actual work, it is ready to send" — but learners may not know what that looks like. Showing the contrast between a vague fill-in and a specific one builds the muscle for judging their own personalisations before they hit send.

**Idea 3: "My First Five" Personal Template Bank**
- Format: Blank template bank — a printable or fillable one-pager with five empty template slots
- Contents / what the user does: Five blank templates the user writes themselves, based on the situations they most often face. Each slot has: "Situation:" (one line), "My template:" (space for the message with their own [brackets]), "Good personalisation looks like:" (one line they fill in as a reminder to themselves). The learner builds this slowly — one template per week, or all five at once. The finished sheet is theirs to reuse.
- Why it reinforces: The module teaches that the pattern works on any template. This exercise makes the learner the template author, not just the template user. That is a meaningful step forward — it shows them the pattern is a writing skill they now possess.

**Preferred pick: Idea 1 — "Fill In the Blanks" Starter Pack**

Because a new learner completing B-02 does not yet need to create their own templates — they need practice at the filling-in step. Idea 3 jumps ahead to authoring, which is E-track territory. Idea 2 is excellent for the concept but a bit classroom-y, and if the worksheet shows AI responses from a different platform to the learner's it risks confusion. Idea 1 is immediately useful and will see repeat use after the course is long finished. It is the thing they print out and stick to the inside of a cupboard door.

**Concerns:** Ten is an arbitrary number. Seven might work better — enough variety without overwhelming. Also: the choice of ten situations involves a lot of assumptions about who the learner is. If it is too skewed toward office work, learners in other contexts feel it is not for them. Would need a mix of personal, professional, and everyday-life situations.

## B-03: Platform Orientation

**Module concept in one sentence:** Know where the main controls are on your AI platform — new chat, history, settings, persistent instructions — so you can find them without hunting.

---

### Supplementary content ideas

**Idea 1: Platform Cheat Sheet — One Page Per Platform**
- Format: Printable reference card — one per platform (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini), landscape A5
- Contents / what the user does: Three cards, one for each platform. Each card covers: where to type, how to start a new chat, where conversation history lives, where persistent instructions / projects / gems are found, where settings are, and one keyboard shortcut worth knowing. Simple language, no screenshots (screenshots go out of date too quickly). The user prints the card for their platform and keeps it with their computer.
- Why it reinforces: B-03 is explicitly positioned as a reference module — "come back here whenever you need to find something." A physical card externalises that reference in the most useful way: it is findable when the browser tab is closed, the phone is locked, or the learner is in a hurry.

**Idea 2: "Your First 10 Minutes" Guided Orientation Walk**
- Format: Step-by-step checklist — printable, one page, platform-specific
- Contents / what the user does: A short numbered checklist the learner works through on their first real session: open your platform (tick), start a new chat (tick), find your conversation history (tick), find where to add persistent instructions (tick), send one test message (tick), close the tab and reopen it (tick), find your chat in history (tick). Seven to ten steps, each taking under thirty seconds. After completing, the learner circles any step they found confusing so they know what to ask about.
- Why it reinforces: The module teaches orientation by description. The checklist makes that orientation physical — the learner actually touches every feature once, not just reads about it. Procedural memory is different from reading memory; this creates the former.

**Idea 3: "What Does This Button Do?" Quick Reference Card**
- Format: Glossary card — small printable reference
- Contents / what the user does: Six to eight terms that appear across AI platforms and confuse beginners: "new chat," "conversation history," "custom instructions / projects / gems," "model selector," "regenerate," "continue conversation." Each term gets a two-line plain-language definition and a note about where to find it on each platform. Not the same as the cheat sheet — this is vocabulary, not navigation.
- Why it reinforces: Some learners are not confused about where things are — they are confused about what things are called and why they exist. This card answers the question "what even is a Project / Gem?" in a way that makes the navigation cheat sheet make sense.

**Preferred pick: Idea 2 — "Your First 10 Minutes" Guided Orientation Walk**

Because orientation is a physical act, not a reading act. The module tells you where things are. The checklist makes you go and find them. The difference between knowing that the history sidebar exists and having actually clicked on it once is the difference between confusion and confidence. This is the one that would stick. It is also the one a nervous learner would most want to complete — ticking seven boxes feels like accomplishing something, which is exactly what B-03 needs to deliver.

**Concerns:** The checklist needs to be genuinely short. If it runs beyond a single page or beyond ten steps, it stops feeling like orientation and starts feeling like a test. Also needs to be platform-specific, which means three versions to create and maintain.

## B-04: Your Input Shapes the Output

**Module concept in one sentence:** The more specific and detailed your message, the more specific and tailored the response — a short vague message gets a short vague answer every time.

---

### Supplementary content ideas

**Idea 1: "Before and After" Response Gallery**
- Format: Worked example collection — printable or PDF, 4 pages
- Contents / what the user does: Four real task scenarios, each showing: the vague version of the message, the AI's vague response, the detailed version of the same message, the AI's detailed response. Scenarios cover a spread of contexts: a professional summary request, a personal planning request, a creative request, a fact-finding request. The learner reads through all four, then picks one that matches their own work and uses the detailed version as their starting template for their own task.
- Why it reinforces: The module already runs a before/after experiment, but with the learner's own content. Not everyone can hold both responses in their head clearly enough to analyse the difference. A gallery of pre-made examples lets learners see the pattern multiple times, in multiple contexts, before they run their own experiment.

**Idea 2: "Detail Dial" Self-Assessment Worksheet**
- Format: Worksheet — one page, self-scored
- Contents / what the user does: A simple five-point scale for each of five dimensions: Did you say who you are? Did you say what the output is for? Did you say what format you want? Did you say how long? Did you say what to leave out? The learner scores a message they recently sent (or one they write now) against the five dimensions. Score of 5/5 = detailed. Score of 2/5 = vague. The sheet then gives a one-line prompt for each missing dimension: "Add [this]" or "Try: [example]." The learner revises their message and sends the improved version.
- Why it reinforces: The module's mechanism is "your message mirrors your detail level." The worksheet makes that mechanism explicit and actionable — not just "add more detail" but "here are the five dimensions of detail, score yourself on each."

**Idea 3: "What Changed?" Observation Log**
- Format: Simple two-column log template — A5 card or printable sheet
- Contents / what the user does: Two columns. Left: "What I added to my message." Right: "What changed in the response." The learner uses this for their first five detailed messages, noting what element they added (role, context, format, length constraint, exclusion) and what changed in the response as a result. After five rows, a prompt at the bottom: "Which addition made the biggest difference?"
- Why it reinforces: The module teaches an abstract principle (input detail shapes output quality) through one experiment. The log extends that experiment across multiple tasks over time, building an empirical personal understanding of the mechanism. The learner stops taking the principle on faith and starts seeing it confirmed in their own data.

**Preferred pick: Idea 2 — "Detail Dial" Self-Assessment Worksheet**

The before/after gallery (Idea 1) is the most immediately impressive, but the module already does the before/after experiment — the gallery adds more examples of a pattern the lesson already demonstrates. The observation log (Idea 3) is my second choice and would be excellent for learners who are genuinely curious, but it requires sustained use over time, and I suspect most B-track learners will not maintain it.

The Detail Dial wins because it is action-oriented, concrete, and teaches the learner to self-diagnose. The five dimensions give them a vocabulary for the thing the module is describing. A learner who has used this worksheet once can say "my context score was low" rather than "my message was not detailed enough" — which is a small but real step toward mastering the skill.

**Concerns:** Five dimensions may feel like too many for a beginner. Could simplify to three: who are you, what do you want, and what does done look like. But then it overlaps heavily with the Opening Seed from B-10. There is a tension between giving learners the full detail framework now and keeping it simple enough to be usable. This is worth raising with B2.

## B-05: One Thing at a Time

**Module concept in one sentence:** Sending multiple tasks in one message produces a scattered result; separate messages for each task produce focused, usable results you can check as you go.

---

### Supplementary content ideas

**Idea 1: Task-Splitting Worksheet**
- Format: Worksheet — one page
- Contents / what the user does: The learner writes one complex thing they want to do at the top ("I want to write a proposal for a new project that includes an overview, a budget estimate, and a timeline"). They then split it into individual tasks in numbered rows below, writing one message per row. Each row has a checkbox: "Happy with this response?" They tick it before moving to the next row. A note at the top: "If you are not happy with row 2, do not move to row 3." At the bottom: a space to note how many tries each task took.
- Why it reinforces: The module teaches the principle and demonstrates it with a specific example. The worksheet gives learners a structure they can apply to any multi-part task — it makes the discipline of checking before progressing explicit and trackable.

**Idea 2: "One Verb" Prompt Starter Pack**
- Format: Prompt template pack — 12 single-task prompt starters on a reference card
- Contents / what the user does: Twelve single-verb, single-object starter messages, grouped by verb: Summarise (3), Draft (3), List (3), Explain (3). Each has one [bracket] to personalise. The learner picks one for each step of a multi-step task, filling in the bracket rather than composing from scratch. Examples: "List three ways I could [do X]. Give each one a one-sentence description." "Summarise [this text] in 4 bullet points. Each bullet under 20 words." The card is designed to sit next to the keyboard.
- Why it reinforces: The friction of breaking a task into pieces is partly creative — learners do not know how to phrase a single-task message. Giving them twelve pre-formed starters removes that friction entirely. The discipline of "one verb per message" becomes habitual because the card makes it the path of least resistance.

**Idea 3: "What Did Each Part Get?" Comparison Prompt**
- Format: Structured observation exercise — a one-page guide with prompts
- Contents / what the user does: The learner sends a three-part request all at once and then sends the same three requests separately. For each part, they fill in: "What the combined response gave me" / "What the separate message gave me" / "Which would I actually use?" A summary row asks: "What changed when I separated the tasks?" The guide concludes with a one-line prompt: "Write your own rule for when to split and when to combine."
- Why it reinforces: The module already runs this experiment — this version adds a structured comparison framework and, crucially, the invitation to write the learner's own rule. That final step moves from observation to internalised principle.

**Preferred pick: Idea 2 — "One Verb" Prompt Starter Pack**

Because the hardest part of applying "one thing at a time" in practice is knowing how to phrase the single thing. The worksheet (Idea 1) helps but requires the learner to already know what single-task messages look like. The comparison exercise (Idea 3) is good but the module already runs the comparison — this extends it without adding much that is new.

The starter pack creates a reference artefact that removes the bottleneck. A learner who has the card can decompose any multi-step task into a sequence of single-verb messages without having to compose from scratch. That is the skill the module wants to build — the card scaffolds it until the habit is natural.

**Concerns:** Twelve prompts might be too many for a pocket card. Eight (two per verb) might be more usable. Also: the four verbs from B-02 (summarise, explain, draft, list) map exactly to this. There is a risk of redundancy if the two packs overlap too heavily. But the framing is different — the B-02 pack is "start here," the B-05 pack is "each step of a multi-step task is one of these." Different use pattern.

## B-06: Tell It When to Stop

**Module concept in one sentence:** Adding a stopping condition — a specific word count, item count, or format constraint — at the end of any message gets you a response that stops where you asked it to.

---

### Supplementary content ideas

**Idea 1: Stopping Condition Phrase Bank**
- Format: Reference card — printable, A5
- Contents / what the user does: A two-section card. Section 1: word-count conditions ("under 80 words," "no more than one paragraph," "one sentence only"). Section 2: structure conditions ("3 bullet points," "5 numbered steps," "just the list — no introduction or conclusion," "4 options with a one-line description each"). Section 3 (smaller): exclusions ("no suggestions I didn't ask for," "no preamble," "no follow-up questions," "no summary at the end"). The learner keeps the card and picks phrases from it when writing any message. They can circle their most-used ones.
- Why it reinforces: The module explains how stopping conditions work and gives several examples. The card consolidates every example into a single reference the learner can use at the moment of writing. The phrases become vocabulary they can grab without thinking.

**Idea 2: "Build Your Condition" Exercise Sheet**
- Format: Worksheet — one page
- Contents / what the user does: Three sections. In Section 1, the learner writes a request they want to send (any request). In Section 2, they choose a stopping condition from a short menu: "I want [X items], [Y words per item], [format: list / paragraph / steps]." In Section 3, they combine the request and the condition into a single message and send it. Below, they note whether the AI respected the condition. A second row invites them to try a tighter condition on the same request and compare.
- Why it reinforces: Writing a condition from scratch is harder than recognising a good one when you see it. This worksheet removes the blank-page problem by giving learners a small menu to choose from and a sentence structure to slot their choices into.

**Idea 3: "Did It Stop Where I Said?" Response Audit Log**
- Format: Simple log — three-column table, printable or in a notes app
- Contents / what the user does: Three columns: "Condition I set," "Did it respect it? (Y / N / Partially)," "What to try next time." The learner fills this in for their first ten messages that include a stopping condition. At the bottom: a prompt — "Which conditions worked best for you? Circle them." Over ten rows, a pattern emerges about which phrasing the AI consistently respects for this learner's tasks.
- Why it reinforces: The module tells learners that specific conditions work better than vague ones. The log gives learners empirical evidence from their own use — they discover for themselves that "keep it short" never works and "under 80 words" usually does. That discovery is stickier than reading the same principle in the lesson.

**Preferred pick: Idea 1 — Stopping Condition Phrase Bank**

Because the most common failure mode for this skill is not conceptual — learners understand that they need a stopping condition — it is lexical. They reach for "keep it brief" instead of "under 80 words" because the specific language does not come naturally yet. The phrase bank gives them the language before they need it. It is the single most immediately usable artefact for this module.

The audit log (Idea 3) is my second choice — it is excellent for curious learners who will persist with it — but the ten-row format assumes sustained engagement that B-track learners may not maintain at this stage.

**Concerns:** The phrase bank needs to avoid creating the impression that there are right phrases and wrong ones. Framing matters: "options to choose from" is better than "the correct stopping conditions." Also, the module itself already shows several examples of good stopping conditions inline — the card needs to add genuine value beyond the module, not just reformat it.

## B-07: What Just Happened? Reading AI Output

**Module concept in one sentence:** Before you react to any AI response, work through four questions to decide whether it answered what you asked, whether anything is generic, invented, or wrong-length.

---

### Supplementary content ideas

**Idea 1: Output Review Checklist Card**
- Format: Printable reference card — small, designed to sit near the screen
- Contents / what the user does: The four checklist questions from the module, formatted as a card: "1. Did it answer what I actually asked? 2. Is anything generic where it should be specific? 3. Did it invent anything I did not give it? 4. Is the length about right?" Below the four questions: three possible decisions — "Use as-is / Ask for one specific change / Start over." Below that: a reminder of the change-request template from the module ("The response is [what it got right]. Please change [specific thing]"). The learner prints this, keeps it next to their screen, and runs through it after every AI response until it is automatic.
- Why it reinforces: The module explicitly says "Keep this checklist. You will use it for every module from here on." This is the module's own intended take-home artefact — producing a properly designed version of it is the natural supplementary output.

**Idea 2: Annotated Response Gallery — "What Went Wrong?"**
- Format: Worked example collection — 4 examples, printable or PDF
- Contents / what the user does: Four AI responses, each with a problem. Response 1: answered the wrong question. Response 2: generic where it should be specific. Response 3: invented a fact. Response 4: wrong length/format. Each response is shown in full, then annotated with arrows pointing to the specific problem. Below each one: the change-request message the learner would send to fix it. The learner reads through all four, then uses the checklist on a real response of their own.
- Why it reinforces: The module lists four failure modes but cannot show them in a real response without making the lesson much longer. A gallery of annotated examples gives learners a visual reference for each failure mode — what it actually looks like in a real AI response, not just as a description.

**Idea 3: "Rate This Response" Practice Set**
- Format: Practice exercise — three AI responses to evaluate, with a scoring grid
- Contents / what the user does: Three AI responses (pre-written for the exercise). Each one has a request shown above it and a grid below it: the four checklist questions, each with Y / N / Partial. The learner scores each response, then compares their scores to the "answer" on the back or in a separate section. A final prompt: "Which failure mode is hardest to spot?"
- Why it reinforces: Reading critically is a skill that needs practice on material you did not write. Using your own AI conversations to practice means you sometimes cannot see the flaw because you are too close to the content. Pre-made examples remove that blind spot.

**Preferred pick: Idea 1 — Output Review Checklist Card**

This is not even a close call. The module literally says "keep this checklist." The natural supplementary artefact is a well-designed, printable version of it. Producing a properly laid-out card that the learner can print and pin up is exactly what the lesson is calling for. The checklist is also cumulative — it appears in later modules as a reference. A physical card has durable value across the entire course.

The annotated gallery (Idea 2) is genuinely useful and would reduce confusion about what each failure mode looks like in practice. But it is secondary to the checklist.

**Concerns:** The checklist card is so obviously the right choice that I am second-guessing myself. Is it too simple? No — simple is right here. The risk would be over-designing it. The card should be tiny, plain, and functional. Not a poster. A card.

## B-08: Recovery — When It Does Not Work

**Module concept in one sentence:** When an AI conversation goes off-track, there are three options — adjust in the same conversation, restate from scratch in the same conversation, or start a new conversation — and knowing which to use is the skill.

---

### Supplementary content ideas

**Idea 1: Recovery Decision Tree**
- Format: Visual decision flowchart — printable, one page
- Contents / what the user does: A simple three-branch decision tree. Question 1: "Did the AI understand the task but get one detail wrong?" → Yes: stay in the same conversation and adjust. Question 2: "Did the AI misunderstand the whole task?" → Yes: restate from scratch in the same conversation (with the restate template). Question 3: "Have you tried adjusting and restating, and it's still wrong?" → Yes: start a new conversation with a better first message. Each branch ends with a one-line action. A note at the bottom: "No conversation is worth more than a new one. Starting fresh costs nothing." The tree doubles as a recovery checklist.
- Why it reinforces: The module presents three options as a hierarchy. For a nervous beginner, deciding which one to use in the moment of frustration is hard. A decision tree removes the decision-making load — you follow the branches, you do not have to think.

**Idea 2: "What Went Wrong?" Diagnosis Prompts**
- Format: Short prompt pack — five diagnosis templates
- Contents / what the user does: Five fill-in-the-blank diagnosis messages the learner can send when they are stuck. Each one is designed to get the AI to explain why its previous response missed the mark: "I wanted [X]. You gave me [Y]. What did my message not tell you that would have helped?" Another: "Re-read my first message in this conversation and tell me: what did you understand my task to be?" The learner picks the diagnosis prompt that fits their situation, sends it, reads the AI's self-report, and uses that information to write a better message.
- Why it reinforces: Most B-track learners will not know what went wrong in their message — they just know the response was wrong. Teaching them to ask the AI to diagnose the failure is a powerful recovery move that the module does not explicitly teach. It turns the AI into a tutor, not just a tool.

**Idea 3: "Fresh Start" Template Pack**
- Format: Prompt template pack — five restate / new-conversation openers
- Contents / what the user does: Five clean opening templates for restarting a conversation, each tailored to a different kind of recovery: (1) when the task was too vague, (2) when the context was missing, (3) when the format came back wrong, (4) when the tone was off, (5) when the AI invented facts. Each template has the structure: "I need to restart. I'm a [role]. I want to [specific task]. [The thing I did not make clear before]. Done when: [stopping condition]." A note at the top: "Copy whichever one fits. Replace the brackets. Send it."
- Why it reinforces: The restate template in the module is general. A pack of five variants, each targeting a specific failure mode, gives learners a recovery tool they can use without diagnosing the problem first — they just pick the template that matches what went wrong.

**Preferred pick: Idea 1 — Recovery Decision Tree**

Because the core skill of B-08 is making a decision under conditions of frustration. That is a higher cognitive load than any other module — you are already in a state of "this is not working" and you have to choose between three options. A decision tree removes almost all of that cognitive load. You just follow it. The question answering is minimal, the action is clear.

The diagnosis prompts (Idea 2) are genuinely clever and I think a sophisticated B-track learner would love them. But they require the learner to stay calm and curious when they are frustrated — which is exactly when many beginners shut down. The decision tree asks less of them in that moment.

**Concerns:** Decision trees are sometimes harder to read than they look, especially when printed small. The design matters a lot here. A poorly laid-out tree will be abandoned; a clean one will be pinned up. Also: the three branches in this tree are not quite parallel — "adjusted and restated and still wrong" is the third branch, but a learner might jump straight to "new conversation" without trying the others. The tree needs to honour that — it should say "you can skip straight here if you want to" rather than implying you must work through the branches in order.

## LOG — B1 — B-08

My first instinct was the restate template pack, because the module centres on the restate template. But then I read more carefully and noticed the module's real skill is knowing which of three options to use. The template already exists in the lesson — more templates feels like more of the same.

The decision tree came from thinking about what it is like to be a beginner with a broken conversation. They are not calmly analysing options. They are confused and slightly embarrassed. A tree is the right format for that state because it offloads the decision-making. You do not have to think; you just answer yes or no.

## B-09: Your Two Projects (Persistent Instructions)

**Module concept in one sentence:** Write your communication preferences once and save them as standing instructions, so every new conversation starts with those preferences already in place.

---

### Supplementary content ideas

**Idea 1: "Machine Mode vs Human Mode" Instruction Template Pair**
- Format: Two ready-to-use templates — one each for Machine Mode and Human Mode, on a single printable card
- Contents / what the user does: Two persistent instruction templates, side by side. Machine Mode: task-first, no preamble, no summaries, concise. Human Mode: warmer, more conversational, allowed to suggest things, can chat as well as complete tasks. Both templates have [brackets] for role and length preference. The learner fills in one or both, saves them to their platform, and switches between them depending on what kind of work they are doing. A note at the bottom: "Two projects. Two modes. One for getting things done, one for thinking things through."
- Why it reinforces: The module provides the Machine Mode template and briefly mentions a Human Mode variant at the end ("If you want to try Human Mode instructions as well..."). Making both modes explicit and side-by-side helps learners understand that persistent instructions are not a one-size-fits-all setting — they are a personal configuration they can tune.

**Idea 2: "What Should the AI Always Know About Me?" Brainstorm Sheet**
- Format: Worksheet — one page, fill-in
- Contents / what the user does: A structured brainstorm with four prompts: (1) How do you prefer to receive information? (bullet points / paragraphs / short / long), (2) What is your role and why does it matter to the AI? (3) What wastes your time in a typical AI response? (4) What tone feels right to you? (friendly / professional / direct / something else?) The learner fills in the sheet, then uses their answers to compose a personalised instruction set — either using the module's template as a base or writing from scratch. The sheet also functions as a record of their preferences so they can remember what they saved.
- Why it reinforces: The module gives a template to fill in. Some learners will copy it without really thinking about whether it reflects their preferences. This worksheet makes the thinking explicit before the copying — it ensures the instructions they save are actually theirs, not a default they did not question.

**Idea 3: Persistent Instruction Library — Six Pre-Made Sets for Different Roles**
- Format: Prompt template pack — six instruction sets, one per role profile
- Contents / what the user does: Six complete persistent instruction sets, one for each of six common learner types: office admin, teacher, freelancer, small business owner, healthcare worker, retired / personal use. Each set is fully written out, with [brackets] only for name and one or two personal preferences. The learner finds the role closest to theirs, fills in the two brackets, and pastes it into their platform in under two minutes. A note at the bottom: "Use one of these as-is to start, then change it once you know what you want."
- Why it reinforces: Composing persistent instructions from scratch is difficult if you do not know what you want yet. Giving learners a starting point that already fits their role removes the blank-page problem and gets them into a working setup faster. They can refine it later — but having something in place immediately means they start benefiting from persistent instructions before they have to think too hard about them.

**Preferred pick: Idea 2 — "What Should the AI Always Know About Me?" Brainstorm Sheet**

Because the biggest risk with B-09 is that learners copy the template without thinking and end up with persistent instructions that do not actually reflect how they work. The brainstorm sheet is a short pre-work exercise that makes the customisation feel intentional rather than administrative. It is also the most personal of the three ideas — learners have to think about themselves, not just fill in blanks. That investment makes the instructions they create feel genuinely theirs.

The library (Idea 3) is the most immediately usable, and I can see it working well for learners who are under time pressure or particularly low in confidence. But the brainstorm sheet has more durable value — it teaches a mental model (these are my preferences, not defaults) that the library does not.

**Concerns:** The brainstorm sheet needs to be genuinely short. If the four prompts generate long responses, the sheet becomes a therapy exercise. Each prompt should have a maximum answer length enforced by the physical space on the sheet — two to three lines per answer.

## B-10: The Opening Seed

**Module concept in one sentence:** Every conversation starts better when your first message contains four things: who you are, what you want done, what the AI needs to know, and what done looks like.

---

### Supplementary content ideas

**Idea 1: Opening Seed Card — Blank Template + Worked Examples**
- Format: Printable card — A5, double-sided
- Contents / what the user does: Front: the blank Opening Seed template from the module, formatted cleanly for regular use. "I'm a [role] and I need to [task]. Here's what you need to know: [context]. Done when: [format, length, scope]." Back: three worked examples across different contexts — one professional, one personal, one creative. The learner keeps this card and uses it to compose the first message of every new conversation until the four-part structure is automatic.
- Why it reinforces: The module explicitly provides a take-home template card at the end. This is the module's own intended artefact — making a well-designed, printable version of it is the natural supplementary output. The worked examples on the reverse turn the blank card from a reminder into a teaching reference.

**Idea 2: "Slot Check" Self-Audit Prompt**
- Format: A single short prompt the learner sends to the AI as a self-check
- Contents / what the user does: Before sending their Opening Seed, the learner can paste this message first in a draft conversation (or to themselves): "I am about to send you this message. Before I do: read it and tell me which of the four parts (role, task, context, done) is weakest or missing. Give me one suggestion only." They send their draft seed. The AI diagnoses the weakest slot. The learner improves that slot, then sends the real message to start their actual work. The prompt is kept on a small card or sticky note.
- Why it reinforces: Getting feedback on a prompt before sending it is a meta-skill the B-track does not teach directly. This exercise turns the AI into a prompt critic, which both reinforces the four-slot structure and models the kind of self-evaluation that more advanced learners do naturally.

**Idea 3: Opening Seed Practice Set — 10 Real-Life Scenarios**
- Format: Practice exercise pack — 10 scenarios with blank four-slot grids
- Contents / what the user does: Ten realistic scenarios (planning a school trip, drafting a difficult email, preparing for a job interview, summarising a report, planning a conversation with a difficult colleague, etc.). For each scenario, the learner fills in the four-slot grid: role / task / context / done. Then they compose their Opening Seed and send it. At the bottom of each scenario: a "did it work?" checklist using the B-07 questions. The learner completes whichever scenarios are most relevant to their life.
- Why it reinforces: Writing an Opening Seed for a fictional scenario is lower stakes than writing one for a real task — which makes it a better environment for first attempts. The ten scenarios also show learners how widely the four-slot structure applies. By the third or fourth scenario, the structure should feel natural.

**Preferred pick: Idea 2 — "Slot Check" Self-Audit Prompt**

This is my strongest idea across all ten modules. The module gives learners a four-part structure. The Slot Check teaches them to evaluate their own application of that structure — using the AI itself as the evaluator. This is the closest thing to having a tutor look over your work before you submit it. It is also the only supplementary idea in my brainstorm that uses the AI as a reflexive tool, not just a task-completion tool. That is genuinely new for a B-track learner at this stage.

The blank template card (Idea 1) is still essential and should exist — the module calls for it. But as a supplementary pick, the Slot Check is more interesting and more powerful.

**Concerns:** The Slot Check requires the learner to use the AI to improve their prompt before using the AI for the actual task. That is a two-step workflow that might confuse or frustrate a beginner. The instruction needs to be very simple: "Send this to the AI first. Read what it says. Fix the weakest part. Then start your real conversation." The framing needs to make the step feel like a quick safety check, not extra work.

## Overall observations across all 10 modules

**Patterns I noticed in myself:**

1. First instincts were almost always "more examples" or "more templates." I had to push against this for most modules because the lessons already contain examples. The more interesting supplementary content asks learners to do something different than the lesson, not the same thing again.

2. I kept landing on reference artefacts (cards, phrase banks, cheat sheets) as my preferred picks. This is because the target learner is low-confidence and benefits from having a physical anchor — something they can return to outside the course context. I am reasonably confident this is right, but B2 may see it differently.

3. The ideas I was most excited about were the ones that used the AI reflexively: the Slot Check (B-10) and the diagnosis prompts (B-08). These feel slightly advanced for B-track but might be exactly what learners need at the end of the track, when they have enough confidence to experiment.

4. Three modules (B-01, B-07, B-10) had their own obvious supplementary content built in by the lesson itself — the comparison card, the checklist, and the template card. The interesting supplementary content in those cases was either making the built-in artefact properly, or finding a genuinely different angle.

**Things I want B2 to push back on:**

- Is the Slot Check (B-10) too meta for a B-track learner? I think it is appropriate at the end of the track, but I am not certain.
- The diagnosis prompts in B-08 — should they go into B-08 or an E-track module? I left them in B-08 but they might be premature.
- Is there value in an "across the track" artefact — something that consolidates all the reference cards into one double-sided sheet for the whole B-track? I did not propose it for any single module because it spans all of them, but it might be the most useful thing of all.
