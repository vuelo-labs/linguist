### E-04/E-06 Overlap: Spiral Signal Structure

**The flag from my brainstorm was correct. Now I need to say what to do with it.**

E-04 and E-06 both produce "checklist of signals" type supplementary content. My E-04 Spiral Signals Checklist and my E-06 Conversation Health Signals card are structurally almost identical — three to five signals, tickboxes, one-line instruction to stop and take a different action. E2 didn't reach for this format as a preferred pick for either module, but E2's Correction Spiral Transcript (E-04) and Over-seeded vs Under-seeded Comparison (E-06) both imply that recognition is the core challenge in both modules.

**The case for keeping them separate:**

The spiral (E-04) and the re-seed trigger (E-06) are different problems. The spiral is a conversation going wrong; the re-seed trigger is a conversation that has run its course or drifted. A learner in a spiral needs to be told to stop and start over with a better brief. A learner at a re-seed point has a different action to take — write a three-part carry-forward — and the signals that indicate it are different (task drift, context decay, completion) from the signals that indicate a spiral (lengthening messages, repeated corrections, the AI ignoring fixes). Combining them risks blurring a distinction the modules are careful to maintain.

**The case for combining them:**

A single "conversation health" reference that distinguishes the two states (spiralling vs. needs-migration) would be more useful in practice than two separate checklists, because in real use a learner often can't tell which state they're in. "My messages are getting longer" is on both lists. A combined reference that says "if you're seeing these signals, you're spiralling; if you're seeing these signals, it's time to re-seed; if you're seeing both, do the re-seed first" would be a more practical tool than two separate cards.

**My recommendation:**

Keep the concepts separate in the module content. Produce a single combined "conversation health" reference card that covers both states, explicitly labelled, cross-referenced to E-04 and E-06. This gives learners one tool they'll actually use, while the modules provide the explanation for each state separately. The card should be listed as supplementary to both modules, not owned by either.

### E-04/E-06: The Conversation Health Card

**I think E1 is right about the combined format. I have a specific design objection.**

### E-05: Get the Plan First

**I want to push back on E1's framing of the Plan Review Checklist.**

E1 defended the Plan Review Checklist (their Idea 1) as a "scanning tool in the gap between Message 1 and Message 2." The four questions are: Does the first step match what I need to happen first? Is there a step I'd need to undo? Is anything missing? Is anything I should do myself? E1's case is that these aren't in my Wrong Plan Exercise and that the checklist earns a companion slot.

Here's my pushback: the four questions are good, but they train learners to check for the right things rather than to develop the judgment that catches the wrong things. The gap between "following a checklist" and "having genuine critical reading skill" is exactly the gap the Wrong Plan Exercise is designed to close. The alphabetical-vs-journey error in my Wrong Plan case is specifically designed to be missable even if you run through those four checklist questions conscientiously. "Is there a step I'd need to undo?" — maybe not, the alphabetical reorganisation wouldn't need to be undone, it just wouldn't work. "Does the first step match what I need to happen first?" — yes, it does. The checklist would let learners pass the wrong plan.

That's not a knock on the checklist — it's an argument for sequencing. The Wrong Plan Exercise teaches you *why* checklist questions can fail, which is a more durable lesson than the checklist itself. Once a learner has nearly rubber-stamped a plausible wrong step, the four questions become meaningful as a framework — they're working from experience, not from instruction. 

My position: Wrong Plan Exercise ships first, as the insight piece. The Plan Review Checklist ships second, as a tool learners reach for after they've learned the checklist isn't sufficient on its own. In that sequence, the checklist becomes more useful, not less — because learners know what it misses.

## Final Consensus

One chosen content piece per module, format, one-sentence rationale.

---

**E-01 — The Verb Autopsy**
Format: Annotated side-by-side comparison doc (printable / pasteable)
Rationale: The physical act of circling unwanted changes in the learner's own text makes the AI's assumed authority visible in a way no other format produces.

**E-02 — The Negative Space Rewrite**
Format: Guided rewrite exercise (15 minutes)
Rationale: Forcing learners to name what they're protecting before writing the constraint installs the protective reflex at the right moment — before the damage, not after.

**E-03 — The Load-Bearing Test Live**
Format: Guided exercise with prediction element (20 minutes)
Rationale: Prediction plus test plus calibration error is the tightest feedback loop available for building the load-bearing intuition — being wrong is the lesson, not an accident.

**E-04 — Restate vs Patch Head-to-Head**
Format: Parallel experiment (two live AI sessions)
Rationale: Running both approaches simultaneously and comparing the effort gap proves the efficiency claim on the learner's own time, which is the only argument that converts behaviour.

**E-04/E-06 — Conversation Health Reference Card**
Format: Two-zone reference card (scannable, single page)
Rationale: Spiral signals and migration signals belong on one card because in real use a learner can't always tell which state they're in — one tool with a tie-breaker line is more useful than two separate checklists.

**E-05 — The Wrong Plan Exercise**
Format: Provided case with a plausible-but-subtle wrong step (guided analysis)
Rationale: The near-miss moment — "I almost rubber-stamped that" — only arrives when the wrong answer is genuinely plausible, and that experience is the one that makes critical plan-reading a durable skill.

**E-06 — The Over-seeded vs Under-seeded Comparison**
Format: Live experiment (three short AI sessions)
Rationale: The counterintuitive finding that more context can produce a worse response than a right-sized re-seed can only be delivered empirically — explanation alone won't shift the "more is better" default.

**E-07 — The Card Stress Test**
Format: Guided usability exercise (90 seconds, one adjacent task)
Rationale: Testing the card under time pressure while the learner is still in the course is the only supplementary piece that closes the feedback loop before the artefact gets filed and forgotten.
