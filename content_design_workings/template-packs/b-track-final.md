# B-track Supplementary Content Pack — Final Brief
*Produced by Opus review agent from multi-agent brainstorm and consensus*
*Date: 2026-04-15*

---

## 1. Overview

This pack is the supplementary content companion to the B-track of the Linguist course — ten modules (B-01 through B-10) that take a complete beginner from "I have never sent a message to an AI" to "I can start any conversation with a well-formed Opening Seed."

**Who it is for:** B-track completers. Complete beginners. Low general tech confidence. Often nervous about AI itself — worried about transgressing rules they cannot see, about looking stupid, about the technology breaking, about pushing back on a response that feels authoritative. Not necessarily in office work. Parents, carers, volunteers, teachers, retirees, and people using AI personally are all in scope.

**How it works:** No technical setup. No coding, no installations, no accounts beyond the one AI platform they are already using. Every artefact is printable, pinnable, or saveable as a digital note. The pack is a physical and mental reference layer that sits beside the course, not inside it. Artefacts are designed to be reached for mid-task — when something feels wrong, when the cursor is blinking on an empty template, when a response lands weird — not only while learning.

**Design principles that emerged from the discussion:**

- **Cards beat pages.** When something competed between a one-card reference and a one-page document, the card won. Pages are for learning; cards are for use. Both formats earn space in the pack, but the card is always the primary.
- **Pre-send beats post-send.** The highest-leverage moment to help a nervous beginner is before they press Send, not after. Preventive tools outrank reflective ones.
- **Permissions before instructions.** For a subset of anxious learners, the blocker is not a missing skill — it is not believing they are allowed to use the skill they have. Naming the permission ("you are allowed to ignore this response") is often more useful than another instruction.
- **Name the fear, do not manage it.** Reassurance that avoids naming the specific fear is weaker than directly naming the thing the learner is worried about and neutralising it. "Ten things that are not problems" works because it lists the phantom rules learners are trying not to break.
- **Return value over exercise value.** An artefact reached for on day 14 is more valuable than one completed once on day 1. When a one-time exercise competed with a recurring reference, the reference won.
- **Pre-filled beats blank.** Small pre-filled elements (a fixed role line on a template card, a version date on a configuration sheet) make an artefact feel personal and owned. Fully blank artefacts feel like homework.

## 2. Chosen Content — Module by Module

### B-01: Your First Message

- **Name:** "Ten Things That Are Not Problems" — pre-send confidence card
- **Format:** Credit-card-sized strip, scannable numbered list, no prose
- **Teaches:** The phantom rules you are afraid of breaking are not rules.
- **Contents:** Ten non-problems, ordered most-feared first. Examples: "asking a question that sounds stupid," "getting a response that's too long," "sending the same message twice," "not saying please or thank you," "closing the tab mid-conversation." No explanations — just the list.
- **Non-negotiable design requirement:** The card works only if it can be scanned in under five seconds without reading prose. The moment it requires careful reading, it fails in the pre-send window it is designed for. Items must be ordered most-feared first so a learner who only glances at the top three still gets the core reassurance.
- **Confirmed secondary:** "What AI Did vs What I Expected" comparison card — optional post-session reflection for learners who want to process what happened. "Your First Week With AI" daily prompt schedule — optional retention tool, explicitly framed as non-mandatory.
- **Why alternatives were rejected:** The comparison card addresses post-send anxiety, but the B-01 problem is pre-send paralysis. A "How AI works" explainer was too conceptual and does not address the emotional barrier.

### B-02: Copy, Personalise, Use

- **Name:** "Bracket Decoder" — translator card for the six most common bracket types
- **Format:** A6 card, six bracket types with two concrete examples each, footer note
- **Teaches:** What kind of thing each bracket is asking for, so you never freeze in front of an unfamiliar one.
- **Contents:** Six bracket types — [your role], [task], [context], [length], [tone], [what to leave out] — each with a one-sentence definition and two examples drawn from non-professional as well as professional life. Footer: "You don't need to be exact. Put in your best guess and send it." The phrase "whatever is most relevant to the task at hand, not necessarily a job title" must appear verbatim next to [your role].
- **Non-negotiable design requirement:** Every bracket type must be answerable by the learner in under ten seconds with the card in front of them. If any example requires interpretation or specialist vocabulary, it fails.
- **Confirmed secondary:** "Good Enough Is Good" imperfect examples gallery — three before/after pairs showing deliberately imperfect personalisations that still produce useful results. Addresses perfectionism as a distinct failure mode and does calibration work (showing what "specific enough" actually looks like). "Fill In the Blanks" starter pack — ten templates across personal, professional, and everyday-life situations, for ongoing practice.
- **Why alternatives were rejected:** The starter pack addresses a symptom (needing practice material) rather than the cause (not knowing what a bracket is asking for). A "good prompt vs. bad prompt" comparison frames personalisation as judgeable, which increases anxiety.

### B-03: Platform Orientation

- **Name:** Per-platform reference card — three versions (Claude / ChatGPT / Gemini), learner keeps only the one for their platform
- **Format:** A6 landscape, single platform per card, spatial language (no button names that break on UI updates), version date, "ask the AI where to find things" recovery path as footnote
- **Teaches:** Where the four features you actually need live on your specific platform.
- **Contents:** Where to type, how to start a new chat, where history lives, where settings are, where persistent instructions / Projects / Gems are set up. Each described in spatial language. Header: "You are using [Platform name]. Keep this card." Footer: if you cannot find something, ask the AI itself.
- **Non-negotiable design requirement:** The card must cover only the learner's platform. Stripping out the other two is a confidence move, not a layout choice — it signals "you only need to know about this one thing." The caveat note about UI changes belongs on this card only, not on the orientation walkthrough.
- **Confirmed secondary:** "Your First 10 Minutes" guided orientation walkthrough — a seven-to-ten step checklist the learner physically works through once in week one. Creates procedural memory the card cannot.
- **Why alternatives were rejected:** A three-platform comparison table is only useful before choosing a platform, and that decision has been made. A vocabulary glossary card addresses the wrong failure mode — navigation, not terminology, is the common confusion.

### B-04: Your Input Shapes the Output

- **Name:** "Before You Send" — four-question pre-send check card
- **Format:** Small card or sticky-note-sized, designed to sit beside the keyboard
- **Teaches:** Before you send a message, check four things — and you will know what to add if anything is missing.
- **Contents:** Four questions: "Have I said who I am? Have I said exactly what I need? Have I said what matters about my situation? Have I said what 'done' looks like?" Closing line: "If you answered no to any one, add one sentence for that gap."
- **Non-negotiable design requirement:** Scannable in ten seconds. Generative rather than prescriptive — the card prompts thinking, not a formula. Language must match the B-10 Opening Seed slots so learners experience them as the same framework at different levels of elaboration, not two different systems.
- **Confirmed secondary:** "Detail Dial" self-assessment worksheet — one-time read that explains the mechanism behind the card (the five dimensions of detail). Positioned as explanation, not a recurring tool.
- **Why alternatives were rejected:** The before/after gallery repeats the comparison the module already runs. An observation log requires sustained use B-track learners are unlikely to maintain. A flowchart for how much detail to add is too much cognitive overhead for what should feel instinctive.

### B-05: One Thing at a Time

- **Name:** "The Two-Verbs Rule" — single-rule pre-send card
- **Format:** Credit-card size. Front: the rule. Back: one worked example of a split.
- **Teaches:** If your message has two verbs, you have two tasks. Split them.
- **Contents:** Front: "Two verbs means two tasks. Split before you send." Back: one example — "Summarise this article and write a social media post" → two separate messages, sent in sequence, checking the first before moving on. The "and" check is retained as a practical trigger ("two verbs often live on either side of an 'and'") but is not the headline rule.
- **Non-negotiable design requirement:** The rule must be clean with no exceptions. Two-verbs framing achieves this where the "and" test does not — "warm and professional" does not trip it. The card must be physically placed somewhere the learner types (pinned, desktop, sticky note) or it will not be used. Placement guidance ships with the card.
- **Confirmed secondary:** "One Verb" starter pack — twelve single-task prompt starters grouped by verb (summarise / draft / list / explain). Solves the phrasing problem once the rule has identified the split. "What I'm Waiting For" three-column review sheet — structured scratchpad for complex multi-step tasks.
- **Why alternatives were rejected:** A "one task per message" scoring rubric is too evaluative. A planning template implies every AI session requires pre-planning, which is the wrong register.

---

### B-06: Tell It When to Stop

- **Name:** Stopping Conditions cheat sheet — twenty examples in four categories
- **Format:** Single page, four visual categories with strong header separation
- **Teaches:** Every message can end with a stopping condition. Here are twenty to choose from.
- **Contents:** Four categories — by item count, by word count, by format exclusion, by combination. Five entries per category. Key value-add is the combination row ("3 bullets, each under 15 words, no preamble") which teaches stacking. B1's exclusion phrasing ("no suggestions I didn't ask for," "no preamble," "no follow-up questions") must be preserved in the final copy.
- **Non-negotiable design requirement:** Strong visual category separation so a learner can scan to the right category in under five seconds. If density defeats scannability, cut to fourteen items (three or four per category) — but design for twenty first and only cut after a real design review finds it unreadable. The combination row is non-negotiable content.
- **Confirmed secondary:** Three scenario vignettes — "what happens without a stopping condition" — positioned as a one-time read companion document: "Read this once. Use the cheat sheet forever." Makes the learner feel recognised before they reach for the working tool.
- **Why alternatives were rejected:** A "when to use a stopping condition" decision guide — the answer is always, so the guide is redundant. A practice exercise where learners add conditions to provided messages is classroom-y; the module already does this live.

---

### B-07: What Just Happened? Reading AI Output

- **Name:** "Four Questions" output checklist card
- **Format:** A6 card, four questions with one signal phrase each, three-next-steps strip at bottom
- **Teaches:** Before reacting to any AI response, check four things and decide what to do next.
- **Contents:** Four questions — "Did it answer what I actually asked? Is anything generic where it should be specific? Did it invent anything I didn't give it? Is the length about right?" Each with a concrete signal phrase for recognising a "no" (e.g. "Would this response be the same if I hadn't said who I am?"). Bottom strip: three possible next steps — "Use it / Change one thing / Start over." Framed as next steps, not a verdict.
- **Non-negotiable design requirement:** The signal phrases carry the card. Without them the four questions are abstract; with them the card operates itself. The strip at the bottom must be framed as next steps, not a mandatory decision.
- **Confirmed secondary:** "It's Allowed to Be Wrong" permissions card — six explicit permissions ("You are allowed to ignore a response and start over," "You are allowed to ask for a completely different version," etc.) and the sentence "The AI does not have feelings about being asked to redo something." This is the item neither agent would have reached alone — it addresses the reluctance to push back that blocks learners from acting on what the checklist tells them. Checklist and permissions card work in sequence: the checklist identifies the problem, the permissions card gives the learner the right to act on it.
- **Why alternatives were rejected:** An annotated response gallery and a "rate this response" practice set are both useful but serve learning once, where the checklist serves use every session from B-07 onward. A hallucination explainer is too easy to get wrong tonally — either it frightens learners or feels like corporate disclaimer.

### B-08: Recovery — When It Does Not Work

- **Name:** "Which Move?" recovery decision strip
- **Format:** Small strip, three rows
- **Teaches:** When something is not working, there is always a next move. Three rows; one is where you are.
- **Contents:** Row 1 — one detail wrong: ask for the specific change, stay in the conversation. Row 2 — AI understood the wrong task: fresh first message in the same conversation, use the restate template, start with "I need to start this over." Row 3 — tried twice and still wrong: open a new conversation, bring only the core task. Tiebreaker at the bottom: "Not sure which row? Was it working on the right task, or the wrong one entirely?"
- **Non-negotiable design requirement:** Physically small, readable in five seconds. If it grows into a page, it stops serving the high-frustration moment it exists for. The strip must interrupt the spiral, not add reading to it.
- **Confirmed secondary:** "When It Goes Wrong, It's Not You" normalising one-pager — five failure scenarios and one-sentence fixes. Title must stay verbatim; the direct attribution is doing the work. "What to Bring Forward" new-chat prep card — four questions for extracting value from a failed conversation before discarding it. Forward-looking, reframes fresh start as a sharper attempt rather than a failure.
- **Confirmed E-track material:** Diagnosis prompts (asking the AI to self-report what it misunderstood). Require calm curiosity that a frustrated B-track learner does not have.
- **Why alternatives were rejected:** A decision tree format requires too much visual navigation at the exact moment the learner has least capacity for it. A "common AI mistakes" taxonomy misframes failure as the AI's problem and risks learned helplessness.

This module is the most content-rich in the pack at three items. Each serves a different moment: strip for acute frustration, one-pager for "why does this keep happening," bring-forward card for "okay, starting fresh."

### B-09: Your Two Projects (Persistent Instructions)

- **Name:** "Five Things Worth Telling the AI Every Time" — preferences discovery card
- **Format:** Small card, five questions, footer directing to the module template. Opening framing: "Most people find they are explaining the same things in every new conversation. Persistent instructions write those explanations once."
- **Teaches:** You already know your preferences — the card helps you surface and write them.
- **Contents:** Five questions that surface the learner's existing preferences: (1) Has the AI ever included something you didn't ask for and didn't need? [fallback phrasing — the "annoying phrase" version assumes prior experience B-track learners may not yet have]. (2) What's your default task? (3) How much text do you usually want back? (4) Bullets or flowing text? (5) Is there something you always have to explain at the start of a new conversation? Footer: "These five answers are your persistent instruction set."
- **Non-negotiable design requirement:** The questions must be answerable from experience, not preference in the abstract. Version date ("Version date: ___") must appear to normalise updating the instructions — treating them as a living configuration, not a permanent setting.
- **Confirmed secondary:** Machine Mode / Human Mode paste-ready template pair — two complete templates, framed as "paste the one that fits" rather than "switch between modes." Personal configuration sheet (Idea 1 from B1) as a place to record what the learner decided.
- **Why alternatives were rejected:** A pre-made instruction library for six role types produces configurations that are not actually the learner's. A persistent-instructions explainer duplicates what the module already does.

Note: This is the weakest primary in the set. The opening framing addition ("most people find they are explaining the same things...") is the production priority — it makes the case for the concept that the module makes quietly.

### B-10: The Opening Seed

- **Name:** Opening Seed Template Card — the graduation artefact
- **Format:** A6 minimum (credit-card only if legibility is confirmed), printable or digital
- **Teaches:** Every new conversation starts with the four-part Opening Seed.
- **Contents:** The three-line template — "I'm a [role] and I need to [task: verb + what]. Here's what you need to know: [context: 1–3 facts]. Done when: [format, length, scope]." One-sentence prompt under each slot. Pre-filled line at the bottom: "My role is always: ___" — the one slot that almost never changes. Header: "Keep this. Use it for every new conversation."
- **Non-negotiable design requirement:** Legibility at size. If A6 cannot hold the prompts at readable scale, do not compromise — reconceive as a pinnable note rather than shrink the type. The pre-filled role line is what makes the card personal and must be on every version. Language must match the B-04 pre-send check.
- **Confirmed secondary:** Five-context example library — complete Opening Seeds for five non-professional everyday users (parent researching a school issue, volunteer coordinator planning a rota, carer explaining a medical result, new employee, person writing a complaint letter). Each with one note on what the context slot specifically contributed. Slot Check — a clearly labelled optional bonus item ("if you want to go further") that teaches the learner to send their draft Opening Seed to the AI for evaluation of which slot is weakest. Placed after the template card, not alongside it.
- **Confirmed E-track material:** Diagnostic card ("Which slot needs fixing?"). Implies regular failure, which is the wrong note at graduation.
- **Why alternatives were rejected:** A ten-scenario practice pack is workbook-y and most learners would not complete it. A scoring rubric reintroduces fear of being wrong at the finish line. A common-mistakes guide ends the track on the wrong emotional note.

## 3. The Re-entry Card

Identified as a gap by B2 and confirmed by both agents. Not a module-level item — a track-level support artefact. Intended for the learner who completed the B-track, took two weeks off, and has returned feeling like they have forgotten everything.

- **Name:** "Welcome back. You haven't forgotten."
- **Format:** Single side of A5 (or a short digital note). Scannable in under thirty seconds. Nothing that requires careful reading.
- **Positioning in the pack:** The first document the learner encounters — before B-01, not after B-10. The returning learner reaches for the pack without knowing which module they need; the re-entry card orients them before they hit any module-level content.
- **Contents — three items with module references:**
  1. The Four Questions checklist (B-07) — "This tells you whether to use a response or ask for a change."
  2. The Stopping Conditions cheat sheet (B-06) — "This tells the AI when to stop. Add one line from this to every message."
  3. The Opening Seed template (B-10) — "This is the full format for a first message. It works even if you only fill in three of the four slots."
- **Closing line:** "Start with any one of these. You do not need to review the whole course. These three are 80% of what you will use."
- **Production timing:** Written last, after the three named items are in final form, so that the language of the re-entry card is pulled directly from the language on the cards it points to. See Section 6.

## 4. The Consolidated Reference Card

A pack-level navigator card. Proposed by B1 and specced by B2. The purpose is to give the learner in week four — who cannot remember which individual card they need — a single place to start.

- **Name:** Consolidated Reference Card (working name)
- **Format:** One page, both sides if needed. Nine blocks, one per module. Each block one line. Designed so a learner can scan all nine in five seconds, recognise which block maps to their current problem, and then go to the full card for that module.
- **The nine one-liner blocks (draft; final language to be pulled from finalised cards):**
  - **B-01:** You are allowed to close the tab mid-conversation.
  - **B-03:** [Platform name] — four things to know: [quick reference].
  - **B-04:** Have you said who you are, what you need, what matters, and what done looks like?
  - **B-05:** Two verbs means two tasks. Split before you send.
  - **B-06:** Add a stopping condition to every message. Pick one: [3 bullets / under 80 words / no preamble].
  - **B-07:** Does it answer what you asked? Is anything generic? Did it invent anything? Is the length right?
  - **B-08:** One thing wrong: ask for the change. Whole task wrong: restate. Tried twice: new conversation.
  - **B-09:** Your persistent instructions are the things you explain at the start of every conversation. Write them once.
  - **B-10:** Role. Task. Context. Done when.
- **Design requirements:** Scannable (nine blocks read in five seconds); each block points unambiguously to its individual card; language is identical to the cards it points to, not paraphrased.
- **When to build:** Last. After all nine individual module cards are finalised. The value of this card is that its language matches the full cards — drafting it before the individual cards exist produces a version that will need to be redrafted.

## 5. Rejected Ideas Worth Revisiting

- **B-08 diagnosis prompts** (B1 Idea 2). Fill-in-the-blank messages that ask the AI to self-report what it misunderstood ("I wanted X. You gave me Y. What did my message not tell you?"). Rejected for B-track because the prompts require calm curiosity in the moment of frustration — a state B-track learners do not reliably have. **Would ship in E-track or I-track**, where learners have the composure to use the AI as a diagnostic tutor.
- **B-10 "Which Slot Needs Fixing?" diagnostic card** (B2 Idea 3). Maps four failure modes to four slots for iterative improvement. Rejected for B-track because the diagnostic implies regular failure, which is the wrong note to end the track on. **Would ship in E-track**, where learners have run enough Opening Seeds to want a repair tool.
- **B-04 "Detail Dial" full five-dimension framework** (B1 Idea 2, preserved as secondary explanation). Scores messages across five dimensions (who you are / what for / format / length / what to leave out). Confirmed as secondary but only for the subset of learners who want to understand the mechanism. **Would be worth developing** as an E-track reflective tool if learner feedback indicates the four-question card is being used well but learners want more depth.
- **B-02 "My First Five" personal template bank** (B1 Idea 3). Blank authoring template for the learner's own most-needed five messages. Rejected because authoring is an E-track capability; B-track learners need to practise filling in before writing their own. **Would ship in E-track** as a follow-up to the B-02 starter pack.
- **"Across the track" consolidated sheet** — this became the Consolidated Reference Card (Section 4) rather than being rejected.

## 6. Suggested Execution Order

1. **B-06 Stopping Conditions cheat sheet.** Start here because it is the most design-intensive item (twenty items, four categories, strong visual hierarchy). Getting this right first sets the typographic pattern for other multi-entry cards.
2. **B-07 Four Questions checklist card** (plus permissions card companion). Highest-return card in the pack — used every session from module seven onward. Signal phrases are the load-bearing copy; draft and test these carefully.
3. **B-10 Opening Seed Template Card** (plus example library, plus Slot Check bonus). The graduation artefact. Must use language that matches B-04 card exactly. Produce B-04 and B-10 in parallel if possible.
4. **B-04 "Before You Send" pre-send check card.** See above — produce alongside B-10 for language consistency.
5. **B-08 Recovery Decision Strip** (plus normalising one-pager, plus bring-forward card). The three-item B-08 bundle. The strip is small but its copy is unusually dense — prioritise its precision.
6. **B-01 "Ten Things That Are Not Problems" card** (plus comparison card secondary). Faster to produce; emotional register is the key design consideration.
7. **B-02 Bracket Decoder card** (plus imperfect examples gallery, plus starter pack).
8. **B-03 Per-Platform Reference card** (three versions, plus orientation walkthrough). Three parallel productions; establishes the version-dating pattern for the pack.
9. **B-05 Two-Verbs Rule card** (plus One Verb starter pack, plus review sheet).
10. **B-09 Preferences Discovery card** (plus paste-ready templates). Weakest module in the set; take extra care on the opening framing line.
11. **Consolidated Reference Card.** Built after the nine module cards are finalised. Its blocks pull language from the final cards.
12. **Re-entry Card.** Built **last**, after B-06, B-07, and B-10 are finalised. Its three references pull language from the final versions of those cards.

**Why the re-entry card is positioned first but written last:** It sits at the front of the physical pack because that is where a returning learner needs to hit it. But its contents are three references to other cards, and its value depends on the three references using exactly the language the cards use. Drafting it before the three cards exist would produce a version that would need to be redrafted once the cards are final. Production sequence: build the foundation cards first, then write the card that points to them. Position it first in the assembled pack regardless.

## 7. Cross-cutting Design Principles

Non-obvious design decisions that apply across the whole pack:

**Cards beat pages — but pages earn their place as secondaries.** Every primary pick in the pack is a card format. The reason is physical: a card can be picked up in the middle of a task without requiring the learner to read something. Pages appear throughout as secondaries because some work (calibration, normalisation, explanation of mechanism) cannot be compressed to card size. The rule is: if it is used every session, it is a card; if it is read once and then absorbed, it is a page.

**The permission-strip pattern.** The most important discovery in the discussion was that anxious learners often already have the skill — they are blocked by not believing they are permitted to use it. The B-07 permissions card is the clearest example ("The AI does not have feelings about being asked to redo something"). This pattern — explicitly naming what the learner is allowed to do — is distinct from teaching what to do. Where learners know the right action but hesitate, a permission statement removes the block that more instruction would not.

**Name the fear, do not manage it.** The "Ten Things That Are Not Problems" card (B-01) and the "When It Goes Wrong, It's Not You" one-pager (B-08) both work by naming the exact fear the learner has and then neutralising it. Generic reassurance ("don't worry!") does nothing. Direct attribution ("it's not you") or direct enumeration ("these ten things are not problems") gives the fear something to land on and be cancelled against. The titles of these artefacts are load-bearing and must stay verbatim.

**Pre-filled beats blank.** Small pre-filled elements make an artefact feel owned rather than assigned. The "My role is always: ___" line on the Opening Seed card (B-10) and the "Version date: ___" line on the persistent instructions artefacts (B-09) are small additions but they do meaningful work. A fully blank artefact feels like homework; a mostly-fixed artefact with one personal line feels like a tool the learner has.

**Primary vs secondary — use context, not importance.** A secondary item in this pack is not less valuable than the primary. It serves a different use context. Primaries are recurring reference tools; secondaries are one-time reads that get the learner to a place where the primary makes sense, or companions that address a specific subset of learners. The distinction matters for production: secondaries should be framed explicitly as one-time reads ("read this once. Use the card forever.") so they are not skipped when they should be read, and the primary is not read when it should be reached for.

**What makes a B-track artefact have return value vs feel like homework:**
- Return value: reached for at the moment of use (writing a message, evaluating a response, deciding what to do next). Scannable. Works without prior reading. Placed where the learner is already looking (keyboard, pinboard, desktop). Addresses a recurring problem.
- Homework: requires the learner to be in a learning mode — sitting down, filling in, scoring themselves, reflecting after the fact. Requires sustained engagement across multiple sessions. Requires prior reading to use. Placed separately from the work surface.

Secondaries that look like homework are fine if they are framed as one-time reads and are genuinely short. Primaries must have return value — no exceptions.

**Physical placement is a production requirement, not a user decision.** The "Two-Verbs Rule" card (B-05), the Opening Seed card (B-10), and the pre-send check (B-04) are all point-of-action tools. They only work if the learner has them where they type. The pack should ship placement guidance with every card of this type. Printing a card and filing it in a drawer is indistinguishable from not having it.

**Consistency of language across cards is a pack-level requirement.** The B-04 pre-send check and the B-10 Opening Seed are the same framework at different elaboration levels. The Consolidated Reference Card pulls one-liners from the individual cards. The Re-entry Card pulls three references from B-06, B-07, and B-10. Every one of these relationships fails if the language drifts between cards. Copy must be locked across cards as a coordinated production task, not per-card.
