# Liam's voice: full breakdown

## Bottom line first

Your voice has two distinct registers that have evolved over time. The early articles (2024) are more conventional analyst-blog with personality on top. The March 2026 chatbot piece is where the voice has fully landed — short sentences stacked into rhythm, almost no commas, fragments doing real work, and the reader pulled along by cadence rather than structure. **Linguist content needs to anchor to the 2026 register, not the 2024 one.**

---

## Sentence structure & cadence

| Pattern | What you do | Example from the corpus |
|---|---|---|
| **Stacked short declaratives** | Three to five short sentences in a row, each a complete thought, building rhythm through repetition | "It waits. It'll wait forever. It does not mind." |
| **Fragment as percussion** | Single-clause fragments after a longer sentence, used like a drumbeat | "The machine doesn't do that. The machine doesn't mind waiting. Doesn't get impatient. Doesn't resent you for going outside." |
| **Anaphora** | Repeating sentence openers ("It's…", "The cost is…", "You'll…") to build a list-by-stealth without using bullets | "The cost is burnout. The cost is strained relationships… The cost is not enjoying your life." |
| **Trail-off ellipsis** | "just…" then a beat, then the verb. Used to slow the reader and signal a thinking pause | "your battery just… empties" |
| **Triple beat with line breaks** | Three short clauses on three lines, rhythmic | "Might take a minute. / Might take a day. / It'll come back when it's done…" |
| **The wandering long sentence** | Occasional longer sentence that piles clauses without commas-after-and, lands somewhere unexpected | "We're these blobs of energy bumping into other blobs of energy trying to figure out if we can relate to each other and maybe, possibly, help each other grow." |

---

## Punctuation tells (the AI giveaways you avoid)

- **No em-dashes used as syntactic glue.** You use them, but mostly as the dash version of a colon ("It's a learning tool — meant to improve future outcomes"). The AI tic of "X — and crucially, Y — Z" is absent.
- **No serial/Oxford comma after "and".** "The decisions, the constant processing" not "the decisions, the constant processing,". This is the cleanest tell. AI prose is overrun with "X, Y, and Z" — yours is "X, Y and Z" or just "X. Y. Z."
- **Commas dropped before short coordinating clauses.** "I started with three simple questions before picking up new work" not "…before picking up new work,".
- **Ellipsis (..) used non-standardly** — often two dots, sometimes three. Always to signal a pause or a thought trailing, never as omission marker.
- **Sentence-final question marks doing work.** "Overhead tasks?" as a one-word question that you then answer.
- **Stars for swearing.** "Sh*t", "bullsh\*t". Self-edited but not removed.

## Word choice & lexical fingerprints

**Words you use that AI rarely lands on:**
- "grand", "proper" (Irish English markers, but used sparingly — not performed)
- "donkey" (as in "tell you you're being a donkey") — affectionate insult
- "wobble" (for breakdown — understated to the point of euphemism)
- "messy", "blob", "analog" — you reach for biological/organic words when contrasting with machines
- "pints", "the school run", "the cobwebs" — domestic Irish-English texture

**Phrases that recur:**
- "Honestly" / "If I'm honest" — but earlier than I expected; less in 2026
- "Spoiler alert:" — used in 2024, **dropped by 2026**. Don't bring it back.
- "Here's the thing." / "Here's what's fascinating." — section pivots
- "That's it." / "That's the whole thing." — closer beats
- "Let me tell you about…" — anecdote opener

**AI markers absent from your prose:**
- "delve", "tapestry", "navigate", "unlock", "leverage" (as a verb in body copy)
- "It's worth noting that…" / "It's important to remember…"
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "Not just X, but Y" as a hinge construction
- Over-balanced sentences ("On one hand… on the other…")

## Voice posture

**Fellow traveller, not prescriber** — this is real and consistent. You do it by:
- Telling the failure first, then the lesson. Never the lesson first.
- Putting yourself in the example ("In my case…", "I used to think…")
- Using "you" but pulling it back to "I did this too" before it becomes preachy
- Closing with a question rather than a CTA

**Self-deprecation is dry and parenthetical**, not performative. "(Not that I've ever done that, of course…)" — the bracket is the joke. You don't sell it.

**Irish-direct, not Irish-charming.** "We're a bunch of people trying to figure shit out" — the directness is the warmth. There's no twinkle, no blarney. The tone is closer to a colleague venting at lunch than a writer writing.

---

## Structural moves within a piece

1. **Open in a moment, never with framing.** A quote you said, a feeling you had, a specific date. The 2026 piece breaks this slightly — opens with "IMO, it's not the work" — which works because the IMO is itself a moment, a guy looking up from his desk.
2. **Mid-piece pivot via short header.** Three to five word section heads ("There's two modes. That's it.", "The cost is real.", "I know because I did it.") — they read like the next sentence, not like document structure.
3. **Pull-quote that's a sharpened version of something you just said**, set off as its own beat.
4. **Land back at the opening.** "Go be human." closes a piece that opened with feeling like you need a nap.

## The shift from 2024 to 2026 (this matters for Linguist)

| 2024 voice | 2026 voice |
|---|---|
| "Spoiler alert: they weren't." | (this device is gone) |
| Bullet lists for frameworks | Stacked sentences doing the same job |
| Source citations in body | Sources stripped or used only when load-bearing |
| Sub-headers descriptive ("Common Missteps to Avoid") | Sub-headers conversational ("It's smaller than you think.") |
| Closing CTA + ko-fi + LinkedIn block | (still present but earned, not pasted) |
| Some hedging ("typically", "generally") | Almost no hedging — declaratives doing the work |
| Em-dashes as connectors | Full stops as connectors |

**The 2026 piece is what your voice sounds like when it's not pretending to be a Medium post.** Anything generated for Linguist should sit closer to that register — short, declarative, rhythmic, comma-light.

---

## Things to push back on if a draft drifts

- Any "X, Y, and Z" with the Oxford comma — that's not you
- Any em-dash being used to glue two ideas that should be two sentences
- Any opening that frames the piece ("In this article, we'll explore…")
- Any "It's important to note that"
- Any list of three balanced items (AI's favourite shape)
- Any sentence longer than ~25 words that doesn't earn it by wandering somewhere specific
- Any closer that summarises ("So in conclusion…") instead of landing back at the opening moment
- "Spoiler:" — gone, stays gone
